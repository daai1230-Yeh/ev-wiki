# The Mercedes VLE Is A Brave Gamble. Will It Pay Off?

**Author**: Chris Rosales | **Date**: Jun 19, 2026

Mercedes' ultra-luxury van could crack Americans' van stigma. But that's only if they don't balk at the price.

---

**2028 Mercedes-Benz VLE 400 Key Specs:**
- Estimated price: ~$135,000
- Battery: 115 kWh NMC
- Motor: Dual permanent-magnet synchronous
- Output: 415 hp / 490 lb-ft of torque
- 0-60 MPH: 6.4 seconds
- EV Range: 435 miles (WLTP)

---

It's a rare thing to see a safe, stable megacorporation take a huge swing. The fact that the Mercedes VLE, an electric luxury van, exists at all is unbelievable. Even stranger? It's coming to the United States.

Mercedes has decided the time is ripe to take a swing at a product category that doesn't currently exist, selling three versions: the VLE, VLS, and VLS Maybach. All of them are fully electric, with brand-new platforms. Despite Mercedes saying it won't hit US shores until 2028, InsideEVs went to Spain to drive the VLE.

Mercedes engineered the VLE as a luxury product from go, using a new all-electric van platform. The automaker upped structural rigidity to reduce NVH, packaged the rear suspension tightly for a completely flat floor, and baked in more sound insulation, as well as soft bushings for ride quality.

US-bound trim levels aren't final, but Mercedes said the long-wheelbase version would be the base van, coming only with a panoramic roof, Burmester stereo, and a triple-screen MBUX infotainment system. MBUX is new and particularly improved — twin 14-inch displays for center and passenger, with major controls like HVAC and driver assistance taking less screen real estate but easier to access.

The VLE is a van to drive — high and commanding seating position, light linear inputs. It feels pedestrian to pilot, but the party is in the back. The second-row reclining seats are the business: squeaks and rattles were nonexistent, whisper-quiet at highway speeds, ride quality decent — unbelievably soft and floaty, but crashes into bump stops on rough surfaces.

Observed efficiency: around 3.6 to 3.8 mi/kWh with a bias toward slower-speed city driving. On an American highway, expect low 3s and high 2s.

Is it desirable enough for Americans at around $100,000 for a decently-trimmed one? For that answer, we'll have to wait and see.
