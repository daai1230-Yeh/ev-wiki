# Ford's New Battery Container Is Here To Take On The Tesla Megapack

**Author**: Iulian Dnistran | **Date**: May 12, 2026

---

Ford has formally entered the stationary battery storage market with a new wholly owned subsidiary, Ford Energy. Its first product, called the DC block, is positioned as a direct rival to Tesla's Megapack and is being assembled in a repurposed plant in Glendale, Kentucky. The site previously built high-nickel battery packs for the F-150 Lightning and E-Transit in partnership with SK On; after the joint venture wound down late last year, Ford retained the Glendale plant while SK On took full ownership of a sister facility in Tennessee. The pivot makes commercial sense given that the Lightning was discontinued in 2025, E-Transit volumes have disappointed, and U.S. EV demand cooled after the $7,500 federal credit was cancelled in September.

The DC block is built around prismatic lithium iron phosphate (LFP) cells with 512 amp-hour capacity, packaged in a standard 20-foot container with all power electronics included. Each unit stores 5.45 megawatt-hours, runs at 1,040–1,500 V DC, and is offered in two-hour and four-hour configurations. It uses liquid-cooled thermal management, a three-tier battery management system that watches voltage, current, temperature, state of charge and state of health, and a layered fire-protection package combining smoke, heat and hydrogen detection with ventilation and suppression. The container is rated IP55 and operates from -35°C to 55°C, and the design target is a 20-year service life.

Ford Energy plans to manufacture everything from the electrode coils through to fully assembled containers, with a projected annual capacity of 20 GWh and first customer deliveries scheduled for early 2027. Tesla retains a substantial lead: its Megapack capacity is roughly 80 GWh per year (about 20,000 units), with more than 47 GWh deployed across over 65 countries to date. For context, Tesla's energy storage business produced $1.1 billion in gross profit in Q4 2025 alone, illustrating why incumbents see this as one of the most attractive battery growth markets.
