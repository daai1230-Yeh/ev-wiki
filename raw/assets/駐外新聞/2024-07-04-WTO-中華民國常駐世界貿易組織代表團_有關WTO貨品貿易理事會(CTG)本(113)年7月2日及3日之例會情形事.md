Statement by the Separate Customs Territory of Taiwan, Penghu, Kinmen and Matsu

2024.7.2&3

Item 13: The Separate Customs Territory of Taiwan, Penghu, Kinmen and Matsu- Industrial relevance policies in the offshore wind sector- Requested by the EU

We thank the European Union for their interest in our offshore wind sector policies.

Offshore wind power has become a key pillar of our renewable energy infrastructure, crucial for our "2050 Net Zero Carbon Emissions" goal and our energy and economic security. Given our unique geographical features and weather patterns, such as subtropical monsoons and typhoons, it is essential to develop a resilient supply chain for the offshore wind sector.

In this context, we provide opportunities for potential investors and stakeholders to identify solutions that meet offshore wind developers' needs. Close cooperation with developers is crucial to achieving our policy objectives. 

Regarding the industrial relevance policy, developers can choose their own supply chain partners. Should developers choose not to collaborate with local partners, they can still participate in the bidding process. 

Our offshore wind power policy is now in its third phase, and we appreciate the interest shown by international developers. Developers from various countries, including EU member states, have provided diverse suggestions on how our government should advance the project. We will continue seeking stakeholders’ input to balance these suggestions.

European businesses are leaders in the global offshore wind power industry and are crucial partners in our development efforts. We will continue engaging in dialogue with the European Union to achieve mutually beneficial outcomes.

Item 17 China – Administrative Measures for Registration of Overseas Producers of Imported Foods – Requested by the United States

My delegation reiterates our concern regarding the implementation of Decree 248, particularly the ongoing delays in the registration process. As we indicated in the earlier TBT meeting in June, as of 6 June 2024, among 3,282 applications submitted by our firms under these measures, only 1,098 (about 33%) have been approved. The rest are still pending. Many applications remain subject to prolonged delays without clear updates or adequate explanation, despite numerous attempts to inquire with the contact point provided by the General Administration of Customs of China (GACC). 

A lack of clear and complete explanations for the denial of pending applications is also a major problem, making it difficult for applicants to take corrective action and resulting in additional delays.

All these have resulted in unnecessary trade restrictions. We strongly urge China to exercise efficiency and transparency in its review and approval process, in line with Articles 5.2.2 and 5.2.8 of the TBT Agreement. At the very least, China should be reasonably expected to inform applicants of the status of their applications and any deficiencies in a precise and timely manner, as well as to provide explanations for any delays. Applications should be approved unless there are valid grounds for denial. 

We urge China to address our concern in a solution-oriented and timely manner. 

Item 24: EU- Carbon Border Adjustment Measures request by China, Russia, and Indonesia

In our previous statement at the April CTG meeting, we indicated our concerns regarding the EU CBAM, specifically potential discriminatory treatment against imported like products and complex implementing procedures, such as excessive reporting burdens, risk of trade secret disclosure, punitive fines, and the lack of EU-accredited verifiers to implement the mechanism.

Today, we would like to emphasize the importance of promoting mutual recognition of conformity assessment procedures associated with the CBAM requirement. We all know that the IAF MLA is an internationally accepted framework for the mutual recognition of accreditation bodies. The IAF has also developed a Greenhouse Gas Mutual Recognition Arrangement, which currently has 33 National Accreditation Bodies (NAB) as signatories.

Internationally, we note that the UK CBAM has already accepted the IAF MLA framework in its policy document. In terms of standards, IAF's management standards for greenhouse gas verifiers (ISO 17029 and ISO 14065) are consistent with those of the EU ETS and can meet the EU CBAM requirements for verifiers.

In our view, the requirements for accreditation bodies and verifiers under the CBAM fully meet the definition of conformity assessment procedures as stated in the WTO/TBT Agreement, as they ensure that goods (e.g., cement, iron, and steel) comply with CBAM requirements for carbon emissions through a structured and accredited process of verification. We would like to remind the EU of its obligations under Article 6 of the TBT Agreement regarding the acceptance of conformity assessment results produced by designated bodies in the exporting Member (Article 6.1.2) and the participation of conformity assessment bodies located in other Members' territories under conditions no less favorable than those accorded to bodies located within their own territory or any other country (Article 6.4).

Acceptance of verification reports issued by verification bodies under the IAF MLA framework would not only strengthen the EU's effective implementation of the CBAM but also ensure compliance with the TBT Agreement by reducing potential trade barriers arising from the lack of sufficient EU-accredited verifiers.

We urge the EU to take Members’ serious concerns into account and continue to engage with Members to ensure that normal trade will not be disrupted when the transitional period ends. 

Item 26: India - Import Policy on Tyres request by the Separate Customs Territory of Taiwan, Penghu, Kinmen and Matsu, EU, Thailand

Since this is a long-standing concern, our statement made at the previous CTG meeting remains valid. We are disappointed that Members' concerns remain unaddressed. Consequently, international tyre suppliers, including ours, continue to be adversely impacted by the measure and even face increased trade barriers to access the Indian market.

Statistics from all sources show that India’s tyre industry has established a robust domestic manufacturing foothold and has benefited from unrestricted markets in other Member countries. This prompts us to question the necessity and genuine purpose of the current licensing practice, especially given its evident trade-restrictive impact.

We urge India to adopt a solution-oriented approach to address Members’ concerns and consider terminating such restrictive measures at the earliest.

Item 33: Import Restrictions on PCs, Tablets, and other electronic products-request by China

My delegation continues to monitor the implementation of the import management system for the concerned products. We note that the authorizations issued to the importers are valid until 30th September 2024. We urge India to ensure the predictability and transparency of this management system. It is imperative that the automatic licensing regime should not create any trade-restrictive effects. 

Additionally, we emphasize the importance of India providing sufficient opportunities and appropriate time periods for traders and stakeholders to comment on any proposed changes. They should also be given reasonably enough time to adapt to any new requirements.

We look forward to hearing India’s update on this measure. 

Item 44: China – Implementation of Trade Disruptive and Restrictive Measures- requested by Australia

My delegation thanks Australia for its update. We continue to share Australia's concern that China's trade-disruptive and restrictive measures still remain in place on certain products. We emphasize that Members' trade measures, whether formal or informal, published or unpublished, should be implemented in a transparent and WTO-consistent manner. Trade measures should not be used as tools to influence the policy choices of other Members. 

My delegation will continue to closely follow the development of this subject from a systemic perspective.
