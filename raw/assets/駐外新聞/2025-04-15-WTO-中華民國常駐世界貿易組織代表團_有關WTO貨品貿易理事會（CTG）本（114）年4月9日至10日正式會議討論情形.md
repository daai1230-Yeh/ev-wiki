## 附件 1

Council for Trade in Goods

Statement by the Separate Customs Territory of Taiwan, Penghu, Kinmen and Matsu

2025.04.9-10

Item 18 China – Administrative Measures for Registration of Overseas Producers of Imported Foods – Requested by the United States

The Separate Customs Territory of Taiwan, Penghu, Kinmen and Matsu would like to reiterate its concerns regarding the implementation of Decree 248 by the General Administration of Customs of China (GACC) governing the registration of overseas producers of imported food. Our producers continue to experience significant delays in the processing of their registration applications, and we remain concerned that the registration procedures lack predictability and are more trade-restrictive than necessary.

As of March 2025, only 1,095 (or 34%) of the 3,230 applications submitted by our businesses in accordance with these measures have been approved, while the rest are still pending. Despite numerous inquiries with the contact point provided by the GACC, many applications remain subject to prolonged delays without adequate explanation. A lack of explanation for denials is also a major obstacle, making it difficult for applicants to take corrective action and resulting in additional delays. Taken together, these failures amount to an unnecessary trade restriction.

We strongly urge China to exercise efficiency and transparency in its review and approval process, especially in line with Articles 5.2.2 and 5.2.8 of the TBT Agreement. 

Ever since China notified the WTO of its intent to implement these measures in 2020, we have repeatedly expressed concerns and sought clarification regarding their implementation through bilateral channels and the TBT Committee, yet none of our concerns have been addressed and our questions have gone unanswered. We urge China to address our concern in a solution-oriented and timely manner. 

That said, we appreciate China’s recent proposal to amend Decree 248, as notified on 10 January 2025. We submitted our comments and requests for clarification on 11 March 2025 to China. We hope these will be carefully considered and look forward to China’s reply. Thank you.

Item 23: EU- Carbon Border Adjustment Measures request by Brazil, China, Indonesia and the Russian Federation

We appreciate and welcome the EU’s recent efforts to simplify the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism(CBAM) as part of the “Omnibus” package.

The move to simplify rules for companies, to ease the burden on particularly SMEs and individuals from CBAM obligations—while maintaining sufficient environmental effectiveness, is pragmatic and a step in the right direction.

We understand that the treatment of carbon prices paid in third countries is also under consideration. Since carbon pricing systems vary—many using carbon credit trading mechanisms that differ from the EU ETS—we encourage the EU to take these differences fully into account. It is important that this process reflects the diverse climate policies of WTO Members.

Additionally, we would like to emphasize the importance of mutual recognition of conformity assessment procedures under CBAM. In our view, the roles of accreditation bodies and verifiers clearly fall within the scope of conformity assessment procedures as defined by the WTO TBT Agreement. These procedures are critical to ensuring that goods meet CBAM requirements through a structured and credible verification process.

We would like to remind the EU of its obligations under Article 6 of the TBT Agreement—specifically, the requirement to accept results from designated bodies in exporting Members (Article 6.1.2) and to treat foreign conformity assessment bodies no less favorably than those in its own territory or in any other country (Article 6.4).

Once again, we welcome the EU’s recent efforts to improve the CBAM framework. At the same time, we urge the EU to ensure that its measures remain fully consistent with WTO principles, particularly regarding transparency, non-discrimination, and the avoidance of unnecessary obstacles to trade.

Item 26: India - Import Policy on Tyres request by EU, the Separate Customs Territory of Taiwan, Penghu, Kinmen and Matsu, and Thailand

This has been a long-standing concern, and our statement from the previous CTG meeting still stands. We are disappointed that Members’ concerns remain unaddressed. As a result, international tyre suppliers, including ours, continue to face significant challenges in accessing the Indian market.

We would like to draw India’s attention to the objective of Article 3.2 of the Import Licensing Agreement, which is to ensure that non-automatic import licensing procedures do not create trade-restrictive or distortive effects on imports.

According to data from India’s Ministry of Commerce and Industry, the quantity of tyres from us fell by 80% between January and November 2024, compared to the same period in 2019. Meanwhile, India’s tyre industry has developed a strong domestic manufacturing base and continues to benefit from open access to markets in other Member countries. This calls into question the necessity and genuine intent behind the current licensing regime, especially in light of its clear trade-restrictive impact.

We urge India to take a constructive, solution-oriented approach and to consider removing these restrictive measures as soon as possible.
