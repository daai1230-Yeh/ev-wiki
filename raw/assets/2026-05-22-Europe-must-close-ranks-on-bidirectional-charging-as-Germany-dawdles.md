# "Europe must close ranks on bidirectional charging" as Germany dawdles

**Author**: Cora Werwitzke | **Date**: 2026-05-21

---

Germany is struggling to get bidirectional charging capabilities to drivers who could support the grid. The German Ministry of Economic Affairs hosted the third 'European Summit for Bidirectional Charging,' where industry partners presented the progress of cross-country and cross-sector collaboration since inaugural summits in 2023 and 2024.

## Current V2G-Ready Vehicles in Germany

An increasing number of passenger cars are now ready for bidirectional charging, including the Renault 5, Ford Capri and Explorer, several Volkswagen models, the Mercedes GLC, and the BMW iX3.

Commercial launches underway:
- **BMW**: First commercial V2G offering in Germany with E.ON (already launched)
- **Ford**: Following summer 2026 with Octopus Energy
- **Volkswagen**: Plans to enter market in Q4 2026 through subsidiary Elli
- **Renault & Mercedes**: Aim to launch later in 2026 with The Mobility House

## Germany's Regulatory Bottlenecks

Germany pledged at two preceding summits (November 2023 and October 2024) to make bidirectional charging market-ready by 2025 — a goal that was not achieved.

Key persistent challenges:
- Pending implementation of MiSpeL (market integration procedure for storage and charging points by the Federal Network Agency)
- Integration of grid fee exemption for intermediate storage (decided but not yet in effect)
- Ambiguities in work-related electricity ancillary costs
- Tax treatment of fed-back electricity volumes
- Insufficiently coordinated control regimes in low-voltage grids
- Over 800 grid operators with uncertain timely implementation

Germany's fragmented distribution grid sector and low adoption of smart meters continue to hinder rollout.

## Summit Outcomes & European Alignment

Industry experts proposed an EU-wide standardised dynamic grid status signal to automatically distribute load and charging flows across all grid levels. Reports from the summit will be submitted to the European Commission to advance the regulatory framework in Europe.

The central message: other European countries are already ahead of Germany. The goal of creating a European single market for bidirectional charging with unified system architecture was agreed upon — but the ball is now in Brussels' court.
