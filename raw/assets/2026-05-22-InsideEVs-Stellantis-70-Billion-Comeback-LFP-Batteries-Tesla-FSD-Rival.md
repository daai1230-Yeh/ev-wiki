# Stellantis Plans $70 Billion Comeback With LFP Batteries And A Tesla FSD Challenger

**Author**: Suvrat Kothari | **Date**: 2026-05-21

*The automaker is giving EVs another shot with cheaper batteries, a new platform, and high-tech chips for AI and autonomy.*

---

Stellantis, parent of 14 carmakers including Jeep, Ram, and Dodge, unveiled a $60 billion turnaround plan spanning new electric vehicles, better software, and higher levels of autonomy — aiming to launch dozens of new EVs and extended-range hybrids on a brand-new software-defined vehicle (SDV) platform by 2030.

## Background: A Company in Trouble

Stellantis has struggled to keep up in the EV and software race. Sales and profits have cratered amid intensifying competition. Its U.S. EV launches misfired — the Jeep Wagoneer S and Dodge Charger Daytona EV both flopped, leaving owners frustrated by unfinished software. Earlier in 2026, Stellantis also pulled plug-in hybrids from the U.S. (Wrangler 4xe, Cherokee 4xe, Chrysler Pacifica PHEV) to refocus on profitable gas trucks and SUVs.

## The New Plan (FaSTLAne 2030)

Over five years, Stellantis plans:
- 29 new EVs
- 15 plug-in hybrids or extended-range models
- 24 traditional hybrids
- 39 combustion-powered vehicles

All built on **STLA One**, a new scalable architecture consolidating five existing platforms into one, covering B, C, and D segments.

## STLA One Technical Highlights

- **800-volt architecture** for faster charging and better efficiency
- **LFP (lithium iron phosphate) batteries** — dominant chemistry in China, reducing dependence on nickel and cobalt
- **Cell-to-body battery design** — cells integrated into the vehicle structure, reducing weight and complexity while increasing energy density
- **70% parts commonality** across segments
- **Qualcomm Snapdragon Digital Chassis** for improved infotainment and cockpit experience
- **Qualcomm Ride Pilot ADAS** for driver-assistance
- **Wayve partnership** for hands-free, supervised door-to-door automated driving (comparable to Tesla FSD)

## Reality Check

"It's a sweeping and ambitious plan. But we've seen bold roadmaps from legacy automakers before that often led to delayed or canceled projects costing billions. When these new technologies start rolling out next year, we'll know whether this is a genuine revival or just another big promise."
