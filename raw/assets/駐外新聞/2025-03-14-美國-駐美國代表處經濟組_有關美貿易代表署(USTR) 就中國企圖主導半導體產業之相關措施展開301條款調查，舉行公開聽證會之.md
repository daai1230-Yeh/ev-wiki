The Office of the U.S. Trade Representative on Tuesday heard from industry representatives,
analysts and other stakeholders on China’s practices in the legacy chip sector and what the
country’s rising profile in that industry means for U.S. economic and national security.

In the final weeks of the Biden administration, USTR launched an investigation (/node/181966)
under Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974 into Chinese government practices it said were aimed at
building a dominant semiconductor sector at the expense of the U.S., with an initial focus on less
advanced, so-called “legacy” chips. Those chips, also called foundational or mature-node chips, are
commonly used in consumer goods, appliances, automotives and defense systems and, according to
USTR, China is on track to control about half of global capacity by 2029. The notice also included
questions about China’s practices vis-a-vis the production of silicon carbide substrates, a material
that can tolerate higher voltages than silicon and, accordingly, is an attractive choice for making
chips used in electric vehicles and other power-intensive applications.

Representatives for U.S. companies in the silicon carbide sector and several analysts told a USTR-led
panel during a public hearing (https://ustr.gov/about-us/policy-offices/press-office/press-
releases/2025/march/public-hearing-regarding-section-301-investigation-chinas-acts-
policies-and-practices-related) to inform the investigation that China was repeating in the chip
sector a pattern of unfair practices -- such as IP theft, subsidization and abusive labor practices --
that it has used in recent decades to gain dominance in industries like solar and others. Such
practices, they said, warrant tariffs, including on downstream products, and other measures to
ensure U.S. makers can compete in what they contend is a strategic sector.

Rep. John Moolenaar (R-MI), chair of the House Select Committee on the Chinese Communist Party,
also appeared, via a remote video connection, and called for implementing “component tariffs” and
ensuring that tariffs apply to “any chip” made in China and used in a final product that enters the
U.S., in addition to working with allies to counter China’s dominance in the sector.

China’s actions, he said, amounted to “state-sponsored economic warfare designed to dominate

cutting edge and foundational semiconductor production.”

Another call for tariffs came from the geopolitical think tank Silverado Policy Accelerator. Sarah
Stewart, the group’s CEO and executive director, contended Chinese companies via unfair practices
in the legacy chip sector were able to export at prices “20-30 percent lower, maybe more,”
conditions she said significantly impede U.S. producers’ ability to compete.

While the U.S. has been more focused on keeping China from acquiring more advanced chip
technologies, she said the country had been “doubling down” on growing its foundational chip
sector. That expansion should not be overlooked, she contended, citing the use of such chips in
“every application” that also involves advanced chips, including those in the defense sector.

Ceding the legacy chip market to China, she said, “means it has control over whether items are
ultimately built.”

Mike Pollard, deputy general counsel for Wolfspeed, Inc., meanwhile, said he believed China was
“well on its way” to achieving dominance in the silicon carbide market. His company, founded on
silicon carbide technology developed by graduate students at North Carolina State University, holds
10-15 percent of the U.S. silicon carbide device market, while manufacturers outside the U.S. supply
the “majority of end market demand for semiconductor devices,” according to the company’s
written testimony (https://comments.ustr.gov/s/commentdetails?rid=RKDDYKT2YM).

Evan Swarztrauber, senior fellow at Foundation for American Innovation, a tech and national
security think tank, contended that existing incentives and protections for the U.S. chip industry
were not enough to correct for Chinese practices in the silicon carbide market. New 20 percent
tariffs on Chinese goods are “welcome” but “insufficient,” he said, calling on USTR to consider a
“significantly higher and targeted tariff” on Chinese-produced silicon carbide wafers and goods that
incorporate them.

Jeff Ferry, chief economist emeritus at the Coalition for a Prosperous America, a group that has
advocated for broad use of tariffs across numerous sectors, similarly urged USTR to consider tariffs
on downstream products that include silicon carbide chips, “especially EV motors.”

Officials sought details on how China was boosting its industry, with one member of the panel
citing reporting that Chinese silicon carbide wafers were being sold for as “low as $500” when such
products historically run closer to $1,500.

An official pressed a representative for ACM Research, a U.S.-headquartered wafer equipment
company that conducts product development, manufacturing and product support via subsidiaries
in China and South Korea, on whether it experienced pressure to form joint ventures in China. ACM
Research Chief Financial Officer Mark McKechnie asserted that joint ventures formed by the
company were “market-driven.”

Joe McReynolds, a representative for Pamir Consulting, a firm that provides advice to companies
investing in China, meanwhile, suggested that some businesses might be incentivized by short-term
interests to share technology -- and that the “exact degree” to which the Chinese government could
be coercing such transfer was less important for the U.S. than the longer-term ramifications of
Chinese dominance in the sector.

An official on the panel also homed in on standards concerns raised by Wolfspeed, asking Pollard to
discuss potential vulnerabilities posed by imports of lower-quality silicon carbide.

While the quality of Chinese-produced silicon carbide is improving, Pollard said, there remains a
risk of substandard material entering the U.S. market. Potential failures resulting from the use of
chips made from low-quality material in highly energy-intensive AI data centers, he warned, could
be “catastrophic.”

Downstream impacts

Officials also heard from representatives of major business groups, including the Consumer
Technology Association, who highlighted concerns about the impacts of potential tariffs on
downstream industries and consumers.

CTA Vice President of International Trade Ed Brzytwa said the group was “particularly concerned”
about what he characterized as “vague references” to downstream products in USTR’s notice of
initiation, warning that the scope of the probe might be excessively broad. The group has urged
USTR to avoid imposing new tariffs and, if it finds Beijing's practices actionable, to instead work to
strike a deal with China to address those practices or focus on export control and non-tariff actions.

Other major industry groups have similarly called for negotiated solutions (/node/182331).

The panel also heard from the National Electrical Manufacturers Association, among others. NEMA,
which represents an industry that is among the biggest purchasers of legacy chips, told the agency
in a written comment (https://comments.ustr.gov/s/commentdetails?rid=WK3XR2TRBY) that
it “supports a reasonable and realistic transition period with a fair and open exclusion process
should foundational semiconductor imports be impacted by tariffs or quantitative measures.”

“It is critical to the electroindustry,” the group added, “that there be no lapse in availability of
foundational semiconductors on competitive terms in any proposed remedy measures.” -- Margaret
