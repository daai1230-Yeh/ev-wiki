# Tesla gets green light for FSD (Supervised) in Lithuania

**Author**: Florian Treiss | **Date**: 2026-05-21

---

Lithuania has become the first EU member state to recognise the type approval for Tesla's Full Self-Driving (Supervised) driver assistance system, granted by the Dutch authority RDW. Previously, Tesla had aimed for EU-wide approval in one go, but that proved more complicated than the US manufacturer had hoped.

## What Was Approved

The Lithuanian Ministry of Transport and Communications, together with the Lithuanian Road Traffic Authority (LTSA), has decided to recognise the provisional EU type approval for Tesla's FSD (Supervised) system issued by the Dutch vehicle authority RDW.

**Important clarification**: This is NOT a fully autonomous driving system at SAE Level 2. FSD (Supervised) is classified as an **SAE Level 2 driver assistance system** — the driver retains full responsibility, must continuously monitor the traffic situation, and be prepared to take control at any time.

Transport Minister Juras Taminskas stated: "Lithuania is among the first countries in Europe where cars can drive autonomously. These technologies can already make a real contribution to safer and more comfortable driving, especially on long journeys or in monotonous traffic."

## Legal Framework

The decision was made in accordance with Regulation (EU) 2018/858, which permits EU member states to recognise provisional type approvals for new transport technologies from other EU countries. This allowed Lithuania to adopt the Netherlands' assessment without conducting its own evaluation.

## Broader EU Strategy

For Tesla, Lithuania's approval marks its second EU country for FSD (Supervised) (after the Netherlands). Tesla is ultimately aiming for EU-wide approval but faces opposition: following a Technical Committee on Motor Vehicles (TCMV) meeting in Brussels in early May, some countries — particularly Scandinavian states — have reservations.

Tesla may pursue a dual strategy: winning over additional 'first movers' while pushing for medium-term EU-wide approval. Without unified approval, a patchwork of regulations could emerge across the EU — a situation particularly inconvenient for cross-border travel.
