Automakers, industry groups call for clarifications of EV tax rules

June 22, 2023 at 4:26 PM

Tweet

The Treasury Department must clarify key elements of the Inflation Reduction Act as the administration moves to
fully implement its tax credits for electric vehicles, say a host of automakers, industry groups, unions and other
stakeholders.

Companies including Tesla, Volkswagen, Panasonic, General Motors and Stellantis, as well as the United Auto
Workers and Sen. Joe Manchin (D-WV), were among those responding to a Treasury Department request for
public comment on the implementation of the IRA’s Section 30D, on tax credits for electric vehicles, by June 16.
The stakeholders highlighted uncertainty over China’s role in U.S. supply chains and the handling of Japanese
automakers, among other concerns, while Manchin accused Treasury of misconstruing Congress’ directive on
where mineral mining processes end and battery manufacturing processes begin.

In its submission, automaker Stellantis, said it was “imperative that the Treasury issues further guidance related to
the Foreign Entity of Concern language as soon as possible,” citing automakers’ pressing need to make
purchasing decisions for 2024 and beyond.

Beginning in 2024, vehicles containing battery components manufactured or assembled by a foreign entity owned
by, controlled by or under the jurisdiction of the Russian, Chinese, North Korean or Iranian governments will not
qualify for IRA tax credits.

In 2025, vehicles with batteries containing critical minerals extracted, processed, or recycled from these countries
will also become ineligible. But Treasury has provided no further details on the scope of control or application of
the term “foreign entity of concern,” the company said.

Several multinational companies pressed the department for more information on this issue, with Samsung and
Panasonic suggesting the creation of a public, searchable list of entities the Treasury has deemed FEOCs.

In its submission, Panasonic urged “Treasury and the IRS to avoid complicated analyses of minority or even
majority controlled companies that are operating outside of the countries listed in the IRA” and called for “an
established and vetted list of concerning entities, like the well-curated Entity List managed by Commerce, which
Commerce can update as needed.”

Volkswagen and Stellantis expressed skepticism about automakers’ ability to fully eliminate FEOCs from U.S.
supply chains.

“The current realities of the automotive industry, the complexity of the critical mineral supply chain, the lack of
critical mineral supplies in the United States and countries with which the United States has a free trade
agreement in effect, and the rapidly growing demand for critical minerals pose extreme burden on qualified
manufacturers to comply,” Volkswagen’s submission said.

Volkswagen argued compliance would be “nearly impossible” if the Treasury department did not permit a de
minimis amount of minerals or components from FEOCs.

China is deeply embedded in global mineral and battery supply chains. The country produces around three-
quarters of all lithium-ion batteries and is home to 70 percent of the globe’s production capacity for cathodes and
85 percent for anodes – critical components for batteries, according to a 2022 International Energy Agency report.

Graphite used in anode material in batteries, for example, is almost exclusively processed in China. In its
submission, Samsung warned of the challenges of securing alternative graphite sources before Treasury’s 2025

“Taking into account the minimum period to set up novel graphite manufacturing locations, Samsung SDI requests
Treasury offer a one year exemption period from critical minerals sourcing requirements for graphite,” the
submission says.

Stellantis and the Electric Drive Transportation Association, a trade association, also proposed allowing de minimis
amounts of critical minerals or components sourced from FEOCs.

Several respondents, including the Argentine and Philippine governments, pressed for clarification over which
trade agreements with the U.S. would allow foreign battery inputs to count toward the content requirements for IRA
tax credits. The IRA limits eligibility for the full EV tax credit to vehicles built with a certain share of U.S.-sourced
battery components and minerals or those sourced from a country with which the U.S. has a free trade agreement.
But the department has said Japanese inputs likely will qualify following the signing of a limited critical mineral
agreement in March.

UAW expressed concern over the prospect of Japanese companies benefiting from U.S. subsidies. The “inclusion
of a country like Japan, risks providing a pathway for taxpayer subsidy to incredibly competitive foreign
manufacturers in exchange for access to a critical mineral supply chain that we do not use,” the UAW said in its
submission, highlighting the limited quantities of critical minerals the U.S. buys from Japan.

Manchin accused Treasury of ignoring Congress’s statutory definitions of each of the 50 critical minerals covered
by Section 30D. He argued that the IRA adopts critical mineral definitions from those outlined in the Internal
Revenue Code, which specifies the chemical forms and accepted purification levels of each mineral, establishing
at what point mineral mining and processing ends and the battery component manufacturing process begins.

Treasury’s use of “constituent materials” to define the final products of extraction and processing operations moves
some processes that Congress deemed part of battery manufacturing to the critical mineral processing category,
the senator wrote.

“Congress has already drawn the line between mining and manufacturing.... It has not left it up to the Treasury to
define or redefine that point,” he said. “It appears that the Treasury has seriously misconstrued the plain language
and clear purpose of the critical minerals and battery component requirements in subsection (e).”

“Either that or the Treasury thinks it has a better approach than the one already enacted by Congress,” he said,
adding that the department “has no such power.” -- Oliver Ward (oward@iwpnews.com)
