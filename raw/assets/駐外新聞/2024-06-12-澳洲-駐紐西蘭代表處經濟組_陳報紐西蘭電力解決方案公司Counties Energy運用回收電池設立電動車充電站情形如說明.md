# 駐紐西蘭代表處經濟組_陳報紐西蘭電力解決方案公司Counties Energy運用回收電池設立電動車充電站情形如說明

**發文日期**：2024-06-12　｜　**地區**：大洋-澳洲

## 主文

/ Energy

Counties Energy: Using old batteries to charge
new batteries

A small Waikato settlement is now home to a new and faster way of charging electric vehicles: from another
battery.

Counties Energy has installed its first Berm Battery service, which features an array of used EV batteries,
after a four-year planning process and two years of testing. 

The low-voltage system at Mercer, Waikato, aims to reduce stress and smooth the load on the electricity
network. The system uses 18 repurposed Nissan Leaf batteries, which are charged at off-peak periods.

The system, known as Revolve, stores 240 kilowatt hours of energy, or about 10 times the storage capacity
of a typical home battery storage unit. 

Counties Energy worked with Relectrify, an Australian battery energy storage developer, to create the
service.

The low-voltage Berm Battery can charge at up to 180 kilowatts (kW) compared with the previous rate of
120kW because of network constraints at the transformer.

It can also be used to charge EVs in a power outage or grid emergency. Two conventional EV chargers were
installed in Mercer in 2022, which were co-funded up to $112,00 by the Energy Efficiency Conservation

Authority as part of the government’s Low Emission Vehicles Contestable Fund.

In contrast, the new Berm Battery was fully funded by Counties Energy.

Part of a wider strategy

Counties Energy chief group strategy and transformation officer Moonis Vegdani said the Berm Battery was
one part of NZ’s future flexible electricity infrastructure.

The lines company, which is owned by a consumer trust, is pursuing a distributed energy strategy to even
out the load on its network more generally. 

That includes the use of smart meter data and real-time network information to actively orchestrate
electricity use.

Earlier this year, Counties Energy completed a study of more than 500 customers, measuring their energy
usage in five-minute intervals. It plans to conduct a small trial this year to actively manage residential EV
charging, rooftop solar, and hot water heating.

The trial is based on lessons learned from South Australia Power Network’s work on distributed electricity.
