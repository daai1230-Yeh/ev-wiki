# California's New EV Incentive Is Almost Here—With Special Benefits For Rivian And Lucid

**Author**: Tim Levin | **Date**: Jun 30, 2026

---

California's lawmakers and governor have agreed to spend $135 million on new electric vehicle incentives. The program will be available only to first-time EV buyers. Price caps are $50,000 for a new vehicle and $25,000 for a used one, but those don't apply to California-based EV companies.

With the federal EV tax credit of $7,500 long gone, California is working up a program to fill the gap. The state's lawmakers and governor have agreed on a new budget that includes $135 million for a new EV incentive for car buyers. Bills are working their way through the legislature now.

The discounts will be available as cash on the hood, meaning buyers and lessees won't have to apply for a tax credit later on. They'll be reserved only for first-time EV buyers, "as confirmed by a buyer attestation." Similar to the latest version of the federal EV tax credit, there will be price caps.

A qualifying new EV's manufacturer-suggested retail price can't exceed $50,000 in order for it to qualify, and a used EV can't sell for more than $25,000. But there's a wrinkle: incentives shall be provided to California-headquartered zero-emission vehicle companies regardless of the vehicle's price, per the bill text.

So a used or new SUV or truck made by Rivian, based in Irvine, would qualify for the incentive despite the fact that Rivian doesn't sell a new vehicle for less than $50,000. At launch, the R2 costs $57,990 for the Performance model with Launch package, though an entry-level version is on the way for $45,000. Lucid, headquartered in the San Francisco Bay Area, will also benefit from the carve-out — its Air sedan and Gravity SUV start from $70,900 and $79,900, respectively.

Tesla was founded in Silicon Valley and still makes cars at its original Fremont, California, factory, but in 2021 Elon Musk moved the carmaker's headquarters to Austin, Texas, so it won't get the benefits of the special carve-out. Many versions of its new cars fall under the price cap anyway. State lawmakers seem to expect legal pushback over the California-only provision.

Since President Trump took office, the government has erased most policies pushing for cleaner vehicles at the federal level. Congress gutted fuel-economy rules by dropping fines to automakers to zero, and the EPA stopped regulating greenhouse-gas emissions from tailpipes. California lost its authority to set stricter rules than the EPA in a controversial move by Congress. Governor Gavin Newsom said the state would step in if Trump got rid of the EV tax credit, which happened in September.

The budget law directs California's Air Resources Board (CARB) to design the new incentive program, including the dollar amounts buyers can receive, and specifies that participating manufacturers will foot half the bill for each incentive. In May, CARB was reportedly targeting $3,500 for new vehicles ($1,750 from the state and $1,750 from the manufacturer) and half that for secondhand EVs.

EV and environmental groups praised the new incentive program, noting that more than 80 percent of first-time drivers who buy or lease an EV become permanent EV drivers, making first-time buyer incentives an efficient way to grow long-term demand.

California's new budget also directs $135 million to boost sales of heavy-duty electric trucks and buses.
