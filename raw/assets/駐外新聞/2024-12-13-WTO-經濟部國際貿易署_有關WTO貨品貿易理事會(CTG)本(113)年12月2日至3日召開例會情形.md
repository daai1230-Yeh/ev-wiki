Item 20: India  -  Import Policy on Tyres request by the Separate Customs 
Territory of Taiwan, Penghu, Kinmen and Matsu, EU, Thailand 

Since this is a long-standing concern, our statement made at the previous CTG 
meeting  remains  valid.  We  are  disappointed  that  Members'  concerns  remain 
unaddressed. Consequently, international tyre suppliers, including ours, continue 
to be adversely impacted by the measure and even face increased trade barriers 
to access the Indian market. 

Statistics from all sources show that India’s tyre industry has established a robust 
domestic manufacturing foothold and has benefited from unrestricted markets in 
other Member countries. This prompts us to question the necessity and genuine 
purpose  of  the  current  licensing  practice,  especially  given  its  evident  trade-
restrictive impact. 

We  urge  India  to  adopt  a  solution-oriented  approach  to  address  Members’ 
concerns and consider terminating such restrictive measures at the earliest. 

Item 28: EU- Carbon Border Adjustment Measures request by China, and 
Russia 

In  our  previous  statement  at  the  July  CTG  meeting,  we  indicated  our  concerns 
regarding the  EU  CBAM, specifically potential discriminatory treatment against 
imported like products and complex implementing procedures, such as excessive 
reporting burdens, risk of trade secret disclosure, punitive fines, and the lack of EU-
accredited verifiers to implement the mechanism. 

We would like to emphasize the importance of promoting mutual recognition of 
conformity assessment procedures associated with the CBAM requirement. In our 
view, the requirements for accreditation bodies and verifiers under the CBAM fully 
meet the definition of conformity assessment procedures as stated in the WTO/TBT 
Agreement, as they ensure that goods comply with CBAM requirements for carbon 
emissions through a structured and accredited process of verification. We would 
like  to  remind  the  EU  of  its  obligations  under Article  6  of  the TBT Agreement 

1 

 
 
regarding the acceptance of conformity assessment results produced by designated 
bodies in the exporting Member (Article 6.1.2) and the participation of conformity 
assessment bodies located in other Members' territories under conditions no less 
favorable than those accorded to bodies located within their own territory or any 
other country (Article 6.4). 

Acceptance  of  verification  reports  issued  by  verification  bodies  under  the  IAF 
(International Accreditation Forum) MLA(Multilateral Recognition Arrangement) 
framework  would  not  only  strengthen  the  EU's  effective  implementation  of  the 
CBAM but also ensure compliance with the TBT Agreement by reducing potential 
trade barriers arising from the lack of sufficient EU-accredited verifiers. 

We urge the EU to take Members’ serious concerns into account and continue to 
engage with Members to ensure that normal trade will not be disrupted when the 
transitional period ends.   

Item29: China — Trade disruptive and restrictive measures- requested by 
Australia 

My  delegation  thanks Australia  for  its  update.  We  continue  to  share Australia's 
concern that China's trade-disruptive and restrictive measures still remain in place 
on certain products. We emphasize that Members' trade measures, whether formal 
or informal, published or unpublished, should be implemented in a transparent and 
WTO-consistent manner. Trade measures should not be used as tools to influence 
the policy choices of other Members.   

My delegation  will continue  to  closely  follow  the development  of this  subject 
from a systemic perspective. 

Item 33: Import Restrictions on PCs, Tablets, and other electronic 
products-request by China 

My delegation continues to monitor the implementation of the import management 
system  for  the  concerned  products.  We  also  note  that  the  import  management 
system  will  remain  in  effect  until  the  end  of  2024. We  urge  India  to  ensure  the 
predictability and transparency of this management system. It is imperative that the 
automatic licensing regime should not create any trade-restrictive effects.  

Additionally,  we  emphasize  the  importance  of  India  providing  sufficient 

2 

 
 
 
opportunities and appropriate time periods for traders and stakeholders to comment 
on any proposed changes. They should also be given reasonably enough time to 
adapt to any new requirements. 

We look forward to hearing India’s update on this measure. 

Item  43  China  –  Administrative  Measures  for  Registration  of  Overseas 
Producers of Imported Foods – Requested by the United States 

My delegation reiterates our concern regarding the implementation of Decree 248, 
particularly  the  ongoing  delays  in  the  registration  process. As  we  indicated  for 
several  times  in  the  previous  CTG  and  TBT  meetings  this  year,  among  3,245 
applications submitted by our firms under these measures, only 1,096 (about 34%) 
have been approved. The rest are still pending. Many applications remain subject 
to  prolonged  delays  without  clear  updates  or  adequate  explanation,  despite 
numerous  attempts  to  inquire  with  the  contact  point  provided  by  the  General 
Administration of Customs of China (GACC).   

A lack of clear and complete explanations for the denial of pending applications is 
also a major problem, making it difficult for applicants to take corrective action and 
resulting in additional delays. 

All these have resulted in unnecessary trade restrictions. We strongly urge China to 
exercise efficiency and transparency in its review and approval process, especially 
in line with Articles 5.2.2 and 5.2.8 of the TBT Agreement.   

Ever since China notified the WTO of its intent to implement these measures in 
2020, we have repeatedly raised concerns and sought clarification through bilateral 
channels  and  WTO  forums,  but  our  concerns  remain  unaddressed,  and  our 
questions unanswered. We urge China to address our concern in a solution-oriented 
and timely manner.
