# 駐美國代表處經濟組_有關川普政府對於中國產品301條款關稅之評估及可能後續措施(含附件)

## 主文

M E M O R A N D U M  

February 27, 2025 

To: 

Taipei Economic and Cultural Representative Office (TECRO) 

From: 

Sally Laing, Eveline Liu 

Re: 

Section 301 Tariffs on China 

This memorandum responds to your request for information regarding Section 301 tariffs on 
Chinese imports thus far and what actions the Trump administration may take next.  Section I of 
this memorandum provides a brief overview of China-related Section 301 investigations and 
tariffs levied against China, and Section II provides examples of the what the administration 
could do in the future.   

I. 

Direction Provided by America First Trade Policy Memorandum 

On January 20, 2025, President Trump issued the America First Trade Policy Memorandum.1  
Among other things, the memorandum ordered the following activities with respect Section 301:  

(1) Section 2(c) directs the USTR in consultation with Treasury, Commerce, and the Senior 

Counselor for Trade and Manufacturing shall review and make recommendations 
regarding unfair trade practices and recommend appropriate action including under 
Section 301.  This direction appears to have been put into action in the Reciprocal Trade 
Federal Register Notice issued on February 20, 2025. 

(2) Section 3(b) provides that the USTR shall assess the May 2024 report “Four-Year 
Review of Actions Taken in the Section 301 Investigation” to consider potential 
additional tariff modifications. 

(3) Section 3(c) provides the USTR shall investigate other unreasonable or discriminatory 
practices by China that burden U.S. commerce and recommend actions under Section 
301. 

Under the authority of the Trade Act of 1974 (“the Act”), the president may investigate and 
address unfair trade practices of other countries.  This is known as a Section 301 investigation.  
Section 301 of the Act authorizes the imposition of tariffs or other remedies after the United 
States Trade Representative (“USTR”) conducts a review, allows the public to submit comments, 
and consults with the foreign country involved.  The USTR has broad discretion in applying 
Section 301 remedies, including the withdrawal or suspension of trade agreement concessions; 
imposing duties or import restrictions; withdraw, limit, or suspend duty-free treatment; or

II. 

Future Actions Pursuant to Section 301 

With respect to the direction in Section 2(c), we anticipate USTR will use the comments 
collected on March 11 in response to the Federal Register Notice to report on unfair or non-
reciprocal trade practices.  USTR will likely identify areas where additional investigations under 
Section 301 or other efforts are required.  As noted in a separate presidential memorandum, one 
area of focus might be digital services taxes or digital trade rules, which were subject to Section 
301 investigations in the first Trump term.  The first Trump term used these investigations to 
pursuant environmental, currency, and other issues.  

With respect to the direction in Section 3(b) to review the modifications made pursuant to the 4-
year review, it is not clear what action USTR will recommend.  The four-year review was a 
lengthy process undertaken by the Biden Administration which resulted in additional tariffs on 
steel, semiconductors, batteries, EVs and other products.  It is not clear what form this 
assessment will take and whether USTR will dive into the substance of China’s compliance with 
the results of the original 301 investigation.  It is more likely that they will agree China has not 
fixed the underlying issues and suggest further modifications to the tariffs.  For instance, many 
of those tariffs had a lengthy phase in period and the new Administration may want to revise 
those time periods or use this modification authority to simply impose more tariffs on China.  
There has been very little signaling from the White House on what they are interested here, but 
when Jamieson Greer was in private practice, he would have tracked this review closely and may 
have views.  For instance, many of the four-year review tariffs were intended to support U.S. 
manufacturing of solar panels, batteries, or EVs.  USTR under Greer may want to make those 
tariffs more aggressive, but we do not have any real sense of their recommendation.  

Finally, like Section 2(c), Section 3(c) required information on discriminatory or unfair practices 
of China that burden U.S. commerce.  This will be an opportunity for USTR to report to the 
president on additional areas of concern with respect to China.  It is not clear what particularly 
they may mention, but activities resulting in overcapacity in the production of EVs, subsidies 
flowing into third markets, and other practices may be a target.  

To the extent that the President simply wants to implement tariffs on China as a negotiating tool 
or for other non-trade reasons, he can do so through either the existing 301 investigation and the 
statute’s modification provisions, or pursuant to the International Emergencies Economic Powers 
Act (IEEPA) statute.  However, if administration officials around Trump would like to make the 
tariffs more durable or seek support from U.S. allies, they may pursue a new Section 301
