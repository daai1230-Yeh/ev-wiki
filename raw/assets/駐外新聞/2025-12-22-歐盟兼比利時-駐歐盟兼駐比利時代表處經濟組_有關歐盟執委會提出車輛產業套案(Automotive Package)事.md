European Commission - Questions and answers

Questions and answers on the Automotive Package

Strasbourg, 16 December 2025

How will these new initiatives boost clean mobility, decarbonisation and competitiveness in
road transport? 

The EU is committed to transitioning to a decarbonised and competitive economy. Decarbonisation
is a powerful driver of growth, as indicated in the Competitiveness Compass and the Clean
Industrial Deal and demonstrated in the latest 2025 Climate Action Progress Report.

The Commission stands by its goal of climate neutrality at the latest by 2050 and is committed
that all policies remain consistent with this level of ambition. Cutting CO2 emissions from road
transport is indispensable as in 2023 they represented about 30% of the EU's overall net CO2
emissions and are still higher than in 1990.

The EU automotive sector is currently facing fierce competition and a deep structural
transformation of unprecedented speed and magnitude, with significant changes in the global
landscape creating challenges for manufacturers. It is therefore extremely important to ensure the
competitiveness of the industry and supporting it in the transition to clean mobility and the
decarbonisation of road transport.  

Following a dedicated Strategic Dialogue with the automotive industry under the leadership of
President von der Leyen and numerous discussions with Member States and stakeholders, from
NGOs to industry, the Commission is presenting today's Automotive Package as a response to these
challenges. 

This package of initiatives provides the first industrial strategy for the automotive industry. It ensures
climate, industrial, and economic coherence. It includes elements and enabling conditions to future-
proof a clean and competitive automotive sector and encourages manufacturers to continue
investing in zero-emission vehicles.

It provides enhanced flexibility and technology-neutrality in the CO2 standards for cars and vans
and a targeted flexibility for heavy-duty vehicles to reach our climate targets and supports the
uptake of zero- and low-emission vehicles in corporate fleets.

The future is electric. Under the Small Affordable Cars initiative, the package offers a supporting
framework for manufacturers to invest in producing small electric vehicles (EVs) made in the EU.

Within the package proposed today, the Commission also creates a new business case for
manufacturing batteries in Europe with a sustainable battery ecosystem in Europe and enhances cost
competitiveness in EU battery production through the Battery Booster.

Lastly, with the new car labelling rules, consumers will be well informed before purchasing new and
second-hand cars and vans with clear information on fuel efficiency and CO2 emissions but also
whether the car is made in the EU. 

The Omnibus proposal ensures administrative, and compliance burden is reduced to improve
manufacturers' competitiveness. The proposal brings overall cost savings for businesses and national
administrations to €706 million of which €222 million for SMEs. This consists of €51 million
administrative costs savings and €655 million compliance costs. This will bring the administrative
savings thanks to all omnibuses and simplification initiatives the Commission has presented so far to
around €14.3bn per year. The Omnibus also delivers on the Small Affordable Cars initiative by
proposing a simple technical definition of “small electric vehicle”.

How will this automotive package drive economic growth and jobs?

The transition to zero-emission vehicles is expected to generate significant long-term cost
savings, supporting economic growth. It will also strengthen the competitiveness of the
automotive industry, helping to stabilise both growth and employment.

The Automotive Package will boost value creation and increase sales of zero-emission vehicles
made in the EU. With annual car production down by 3 million and small car sales down by 1.6
million over the past six years, reviving this segment and supporting the uptake of electric vehicles
made in the EU will support production and jobs in the EU, and make electric mobility more affordable
for European citizens.

The Automotive Omnibus will save vehicle manufacturers €2.1 billion over three years, freeing
resources for innovation and new electric models, strengthening growth in the industry. In addition,
the revised CO2 standards address the supply side, encouraging manufacturers to bring zero-emission
vehicles to the market, while the corporate vehicles proposal targets address demand, particularly in
segments where emissions reductions are most significant. Together, these proposals will support
economic growth and the competitiveness of the EU's automotive industry.

CO2 EMISSION STANDARDS

What are the new CO2 emission standards and flexibilities proposed for cars and vans?

The CO2 emission standards for cars and vans are an essential element of the basket of EU
measures to drive a gradual transition towards zero-emission vehicles, while allowing for sufficient
lead time for a fair transition.

The updated CO2 emission standards for cars and vans maintain that clear direction of travel
towards clean mobility, while providing industry more options to achieve its CO2 targets with
enhanced technology neutrality and flexibilities. Keeping this clear direction will be important for
the industry as a whole and for all actors of the zero-emission value chain and the related ecosystem,
such as battery manufacturers and charging operators.

The proposed revision remains in line with the EU's climate ambition and continues to give a clear
long-term certainty and predictability for investors along the value chain supporting the EU
automotive sector's competitiveness.  Several flexibilities are provided up to and beyond 2035 to
facilitate compliance.

From 2035 onwards, carmakers will need to comply with a 90% tailpipe emissions reduction
target, while the remaining 10% emissions will need to be compensated through  two
compensation mechanisms counting the use of low-carbon steel made in the Union and the
actual emissions savings from e-fuels and biofuels put on the market during a specific year.

E-fuels and biofuels will therefore contribute to the decarbonisation of overall road transport. The
greenhouse gas emission savings from the use of such fuels are accounted as fuel credits for
manufacturers, which may compensate the tailpipe emissions of their vehicles. These fuel credits can
contribute up to 3% of the 2021 reference target.

Low-carbon steel is essential to achieve the EU's climate objectives and to strengthen the
competitiveness of its industrial base. The automotive sector is a key user of steel. The amendment
allows vehicle manufacturers to compensate the CO2 emissions of their vehicles by obtaining credits
for the use of low-carbon steel made in the Union in the vehicles they produce. These low-carbon
steel credits can contribute up to 7% of the 2021 reference targets. This flexibility will incentivise
the use of low-carbon steel in vehicle manufacturing and thus help to create a lead market for low-
carbon steel production in the Union.

The proposal will therefore allow for plug-in hybrids (PHEV), range extenders, mild hybrids, and
internal combustion engine vehicles to still play a role beyond 2035, in addition to full electric
(EVs) and hydrogen vehicles.

In the years up to 2035, the proposal recognises the structural near-term challenges to the uptake
of electric vans and adjusts the 2030 CO2 target from 50% to 40% emission reduction. Flexibility
is also provided to car manufacturers on the counting of small affordable electric cars made in

the EU for CO2 target compliance. By granting them “super credits”, instead of counting as 1 those
will be counted as 1.3, it will incentivise the production of such small EVs made in the EU.

In the period 2030-2032, multiannual compliance or  “banking and borrowing” flexibility is
introduced so that manufacturers may exceed the target in a year if this exceedance is compensated
by an overachievement of the target in another year of the period.  

Why are you also proposing a targeted amendment to the CO 2 standards for heavy-duty
vehicles?

In addition to cars and vans, the Commission proposes a targeted amendment to the CO2 standards
for heavy-duty vehicles. It aims to provide manufacturers of lorries and coaches with an additional
flexibility to ease the compliance of their 2030 CO2 emissions targets applicable. 

The targeted amendment allows manufacturers to collect more emission credits in the years
before 2030 than in the current Regulation. While the Regulation currently allows manufacturers to
gain credits only when their CO2 emissions are below a linear CO 2 emissions reduction trajectory,
with the proposal they would be able to generate credits as soon as their CO2 emissions are below
their annual CO2 emission target.

Beyond facilitating the compliance with the 2030 targets applicable, the flexibility provides the
necessary predictability for manufacturers and incentivise the earlier deployment of zero-emission
heavy-duty vehicles, while maintaining the overall ambition of the CO2 standards.

What are the proposed changes to vehicle labelling rules and how will they help
consumers?

The new proposal updates the rules of the 1999 Car Labelling Directive to ensure that consumers
receive adequate information to make informed purchase decisions when buying vehicles –
which will also support manufacturers in complying with their CO2 targets.

Labels informing consumers on the CO 2 and energy performance of the vehicle will be
displayed both in showrooms and online, and will now be harmonised at EU level, building on the
design of the well-known EU energy labels, which are well recognised by the public. 

The label will now contain new information on the electric energy consumption and the range of
electric vehicles. These new elements will ensure that potential buyers of zero-emission vehicles
receive relevant information to make informed purchase decisions.  It also extends the scope of the
vehicle label, currently limited to new cars, to also include new vans, and second-hand cars and
vans. 

Finally, the proposal makes additional information available in an online product database, to enable
consumers to compare different vehicle models. It also removes the obligation to produce posters and
guides on paper – a simplification reducing costs for manufacturers and dealers. 

CORPORATE VEHICLES

Why is the Commission proposing to decarbonise corporate vehicles?

Corporate vehicles operated by large companies have significant potential to boost the demand for
zero- and low-emission vehicles, bolster the competitiveness of the European automotive industry,
and reduce transport emissions. This is due to their large share of new vehicle registrations and
operational characteristics. In the EU, corporate registrations account for around 60% of all car
registrations and around 90% for van registrations.

The proposed regulation mandates Member States to ensure that a specific share of new
corporate car and van registrations by large companies in their territory is zero- or low-
emission from 2030 onwards. It aims to support the uptake of zero- and low-emission vehicles in
corporate vehicles and drive the transition towards clean mobility. By providing market certainty and

stimulating demand, the proposal will help European automotive manufacturers to strengthen their
global competitiveness, while reducing emissions and fossil fuel dependence in the road transport
sector.

By focusing on large companies, the proposal leverages their purchasing power, giving
manufacturers more certainty about demand for zero- and low-emission vehicles. Because
corporate vehicles are used more intensively than privately owned cars, they accumulate mileage
faster and enter the second-hand market sooner. This increases the availability of affordable zero-
and low-emission vehicles for private buyers while reducing overall transport emissions.

Supporting the uptake of zero- and low-emission corporate vehicles across the EU requires a swift
and consistent approach. Therefore, a regulation is the most effective way to achieve this, as it allows
targets to apply immediately in Member States, creating early market impact and providing
predictability for investment decisions.

It will also make zero- or low- emissions and “made in the EU” a pre-requisite for vehicles benefitting
from public financial support.

What are the new rules set for corporate vehicles?

The proposed regulation introduces mandatory targets for the share of zero-and low-emission
vehicles in new corporate cars and vans registered by large companies in each Member State.
Large companies are defined according to Directive 2013/34/EU.

From 2030 onwards, Member States must ensure that a minimum share of new corporate cars and
vans registered by large companies are zero- or low-emission, with a separate sub-target for zero-
emission vehicles. These targets differ by Member State, reflecting differences in market maturity and
national circumstances across the EU] . Member States must report to the Commission on the total
number of corporate vehicles registered by large companies, including the share of zero- and low
emission vehicles. This information must be reported separately for cars and vans.

To further support the transition towards clean mobility, the proposed Regulation requires that
Member States must not provide any financial incentives for the purchase, lease, rental, hire-
purchase, or operation of corporate vehicles other than low- or zero-emission vehicles that are made
in the EU.

BATTERY BOOSTER

What is the scope of the Battery Booster and how does it relate to other EU initiatives?

With €1.8 billion, the Battery Booster accelerates the development of EU-made batteries by
addressing chokepoints across the entire value chain and supporting the full battery ecosystem. As
part of the Battery Booster, €1.5 billion will support European battery cell producers through interest-
free loans.

The Booster strengthens Europe's upstream supply of materials, provides financial support for
investment and innovation, ensures a level playing field for EU-made batteries, and aligns actions
across Member States to boost competitiveness and sustainability.

It is the EU's comprehensive industrial strategy on batteries, building on initiatives such as the Net
Zero Industry Act, the Automotive Action Plan and the Strategic Action Plan for Batteries. By
supporting investment, creating demand for EU-made batteries, aligning foreign investments with
strategic interests, and fostering innovation and coordination, it adds clear value to the European
battery industry.

AUTOMOTIVE OMNIBUS

How are you reducing compliance and administrative burdens and costs for the Automotive
sector?

In her political guidelines for the European Commission's 2024–2029, President von der Leyen

outlined a vision focused on sustainable prosperity and strengthening competitiveness across Europe,
emphasising the need to make doing business faster and easier.

In this context, the Automotive Omnibus package is expected to bring significant relief to businesses,
with estimated annual savings of approximately €706 million per year  in compliance and
administrative costs. This will bring the administrative savings thanks to all omnibuses and
simplification initiatives the Commission has presented so far to around €14.3 billion per year. The
Automotive Omnibus will achieve these savings through a series of targeted amendments to existing
Regulations.

Amendments to Regulation (EC) No 561/2006 and Regulation (EU) No 165/2014  to allow
Member States to exempt e-Vans with GVWs between 3.5 tonnes and 4.25 tonnes
engaged exclusively in domestic transport from the obligation to install smart
tachographs, thereby putting them on equal footing with their fossil fuel equivalent with the
same payload, and to alleviate the adjustment costs faced by citizens and manufacturers due to
the current ambiguity resulting from divergent national interpretations of recent court rulings.

Amendments to Regulation (EU) 2019/2144, to exempt e-Vans with GVWs between 3.5
tonnes and 4.25 tonnes (classed as N2 vans) from the obligation to be equipped with
speed limitation devices, thereby putting them on equal footing with their fossil fuel
equivalent, N1 diesel vans.

Amendments to Regulation (EC) 2024/1257 to remove Type 6 low-temperature laboratory
test from the Euro 7 framework that duplicate requirements without additional
environmental benefit; to refer to ‘vehicle categories' rather than ‘vehicle types' for Euro 7
engine (Separate Technical Unit) type-approval; and for the development of on-board fuel
and electric energy consumption monitoring (OBFCM) devices and on-board
monitoring (OBM) data infrastructure and common methods for its processing that will help
fully realise the potential of OBM and OBFCM data.

Repeal of Regulation (EU) 540/2014, to remove a competitive disadvantage for EU car
manufacturers when selling their cars globally by preventing them from having to demonstrate
administrative compliance with two sets of requirements on noise.

Amendments to Regulation (EU) 2018/858 introducing a new sub-category of passenger cars,
small electric vehicles of category M1 (‘M1E'), based on a maximum length of 4.2 metres.

What is the potential of these measures to promote the market uptake of small affordable
electric cars in the EU?

Prior to 2035, car manufacturers will be able to benefit from “super credits”, as incorporated in the
CO2 standards, for small affordable electric cars made in the European Union. It provides a strong
incentive for vehicle manufacturers to produce and commercialise higher volumes of small electric
vehicles, with an expected indirect positive effect also on the affordability of these vehicles.

The Omnibus also introduces a new vehicle category under the Small Affordable Cars initiative,
covering electric vehicles up to 4.2 meters in length. The Commission will endeavour to freeze
requirements for 10 years for this category.

This harmonised definition of small electric cars will simplify the work of Member States and local
authorities when setting up fiscal (i.e., purchase subsidies, taxes, road-toll exemption) and non-fiscal
(i.e., size-based parking fees, discounted charging, privileged access to lanes or parking) schemes
aimed at promoting the adoption of small affordable electric vehicles.

European Commission - Press release

Commission takes action for clean and competitive automotive sector

Strasbourg, 16 December 2025

The Commission today presented the Automotive Package to support the sector's efforts in the
transition to clean mobility. It sets an ambitious yet pragmatic policy framework to ensure 2050
climate neutrality and strategic independence while providing more flexibility to manufacturers. It also
responds to calls by EU industry to simplify rules.

The automotive sector has been key to Europe's industrial strength for decades, sustaining millions
of jobs and driving technological innovation. As the world is changing, the car industry is
transforming through new technologies and actors.

Today's package maintains a strong market signal for zero-emission vehicles (ZEV) while
giving the industry more flexibility to achieve CO2 targets, and supports vehicles and batteries
made in the European Union. The corporate vehicles initiative will support the uptake of zero- and
low-emission vehicles. The automotive omnibus enhances competitiveness by saving costs, expected
to be approximately €706 million per year, and cutting red tape, while providing greater
investment certainty.

Commission President von der Leyen said: “Innovation. Clean mobility. Competitiveness. This year,
these were top priorities in our intense dialogues with automotive sector, civil society organisations
and stakeholders. And today, we are addressing them all together. As technology rapidly transforms
mobility and geopolitics reshapes global competition, Europe remains at the forefront of the global
clean transition.” 

Staying the course towards clean mobility with pragmatism

The Commission presents a package that addresses both supply and demand of the automotive
sector's transition: on the supply side, it presents a review of the existing CO2 emission
standards for cars and vans and a targeted amendment to those for heavy-duty vehicles
(HDVs). On the demand side, it proposes an initiative to decarbonise corporate vehicles with
binding national targets for zero- and low-emission vehicles.

The CO2 standards now provide further flexibilities to support the industry and  enhance
technological neutrality, while providing predictability to manufacturers and maintaining clear
market signal towards electrification.

From 2035 onwards, carmakers will need to comply with a 90% tailpipe emissions reduction
target, while the remaining 10% emissions will need to be compensated through the use of low-
carbon steel Made in the Union, or from e-fuels and biofuels.

This will allow for plug-in hybrids (PHEV), range extenders, mild hybrids, and internal
combustion engine vehicles to still play a role beyond 2035, in addition to full electric (EVs)
and hydrogen vehicles.

Prior to 2035, car manufacturers will be able to benefit from “super credits” for small affordable
electric cars made in the European Union. This will incentivise the deployment on the market of
more small EV models. For the 2030 target for cars and vans, additional flexibility is introduced by
allowing “banking & borrowing” for 2030-2032. An additional flexibility is granted for the vans
segment, where the electric vehicle uptake has been structurally more difficult, with a reduction of
the 2030 CO2 vans target from 50% to 40%.

The Commission is also proposing a targeted amendment to the CO2 emission standards for
heavy-duty vehicles with a flexibility easing the compliance with the 2030 targets.

Regarding corporate vehicles, mandatory targets are set at the Member State level to support

the zero- and low-emission vehicle uptake by large companies. Having more zero- and low-emission
vehicles on the market, both first- and second-hand markets – will benefit all customers. As
companies' cars cover higher yearly mileages, it also means more emission reductions. It will also
make zero- or low- emissions and “Made in the EU” a pre-requisite for vehicles benefitting from public
financial support. 

Strengthening Europe's own battery industry

With €1.8 billion, the Battery Booster will accelerate the development of a fully EU-made battery
value chain. As part of the Battery Booster, €1.5 billion will support European battery cell
producers through interest-free loans. Additional targeted policy measures will support
investments, create a European battery value chain and foster innovation and coordination across
Member States. These measures will enhance the cost competitiveness of the sector, secure upstream
supply chains and support sustainable and resilient production in the EU, contributing to the derisking
from dominant global market players. 

Less red tape and stronger enabling conditions for the transition

The Automotive Omnibus will ease administrative burden and cut costs for European
manufacturers, boosting their global competitiveness, and freeing up resources for decarbonisation.
Businesses are expected to save approximately €706 million per year, bringing the administrative
savings thanks to all omnibuses and simplification initiatives the Commission has presented so far to
around €14.3 billion per year. Among other things, it proposes to reduce the number of secondary
legislation that will be adopted in the upcoming years and to streamline testing for new passenger
vans and trucks. This will reduce costs while maintaining highest environmental and safety standards.
The roll-out of electric vans in domestic transport is supported by measures that place them on an
equal footing with internal combustion vans regarding drivers' rest times and rules.

The Omnibus also introduces a new vehicle category under the Small Affordable Cars initiative,
covering electric vehicles up to 4.2 meters in length. This will enable Member States and local
authorities to develop targeted incentives, stimulating demand for small EVs made in the EU.

The Commission is also updating and harmonising car labelling rules, for customers to have
complete information about the cars' emissions when making purchases.

 Background

Today's proposals build on the Automotive Action Plan, and input from industry and key
stakeholders gathered during the Strategic Dialogue under President von der Leyen's leadership
since January 2025.

In January 2025, President von der Leyen launched a Strategic Dialogue on the Future of the
Automotive Industry, bringing together industry representatives, social partners, Member States,
regions and civil society. Three Dialogue meetings have taken place to date, providing a platform to
discuss the challenges and opportunities the sector faces.
