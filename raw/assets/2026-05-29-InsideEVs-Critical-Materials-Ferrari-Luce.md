# Critical Materials — The Ferrari Luce exposes a big problem

**Author**: Mack Hogan et al. | **Date**: 2026-05-29 (InsideEVs: Critical Materials weekly)

---

## Plug In: Who Cares About Performance EVs?

Mack Hogan argues that neither traditional car enthusiasts nor EV enthusiasts seem to care much about electric performance cars. Based on InsideEVs traffic, readers prefer Hyundais and Chevies over Porsches and Ferraris. This leaves Ferrari and other sports-car brands with major problems: their traditional enthusiast bases aren't interested in (or are opposed to) EVs; EV enthusiasts focus on affordable performance and tech rather than boundary-pushing hypercars; and regulations push electrification even though EVs are heavier and, to traditional buyers, less exciting.

Combined with the Ferrari Luce's controversial design (penned by Jony Ive's firm), this explains the widespread scorn. Hogan likes the design and notes the 1,035-horsepower quad-motor car with fully adaptive suspension should be fun to drive. To get enthusiasts excited about EVs, he argues three things must happen: buyers need time to warm up; companies must innovate with performance features gas cars can't match (independent four-motor control, drift modes, tank turns, torque vectoring); and EVs must get lighter (the Luce weighs nearly 5,000 pounds).

## One More Thing: Mercedes-AMG's Silicon Anode Breakthrough

The latest Mercedes-AMG GT 4-Door Coupe ditches the V8 for an all-electric powertrain with cutting-edge battery tech. It arrives later this year as the fastest-charging EV in America: peak 600 kW, 10–80% in just 11 minutes. Key specs: 106 kWh usable capacity, 300+ miles range, 250 miles added in 10 minutes, energy density of 298 Wh/kg. The anode contains silicon; the cathode uses NCMA chemistry.

Silicon anodes reduce reliance on China's graphite supply chain and improve performance (silicon stores far more electrons than graphite), though silicon remains niche, expensive, and prone to swelling and reduced lifespan. Managing heat at 600 kW required one of the most comprehensive cooling systems in an EV: slim cylindrical cells (4.1 in tall, 1 in diameter), coolant flowing evenly around all 2,660 cells, "on-demand cooling" targeting hot spots by module, and a central coolant hub. The real test will be longevity, and the hope is the tech trickles down to mass-market models.

## Driver's Seat: Geely EX2

The Geely EX2 (Xingyuan) is China's best-selling car overall, beating the BYD Seagull and Tesla Model Y. Geely sold roughly 465,000 units last year vs. the Model Y's 425,000. Compared to the Seagull, the EX2 has more space (seats five vs. four), rear-wheel drive, extra cargo room and a frunk — all for about $10,000. The reviewed example had an optional 114-hp motor and 40 kWh battery, feeling quicker than the Seagull though with inferior steering/handling. Unlikely to reach the U.S., but already exported to markets like Malaysia, Brazil and Australia.
