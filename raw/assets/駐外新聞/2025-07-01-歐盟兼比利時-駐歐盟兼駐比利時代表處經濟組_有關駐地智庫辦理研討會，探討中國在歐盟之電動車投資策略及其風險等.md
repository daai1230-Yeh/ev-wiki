## 附件 1

Full throttle or brake? A smart strategy for Chinese EV investments 
in Europe 

24/06/25 

1. Context and opening remarks – Simone Tagliapietra (Bruegel Senior Fellow) 

The  decarbonization  of  Europe’s  transport  sector  is  urgent  if  the  EU  is  to  meet  its 
climate goals. Electric vehicles (EVs) are central to this transition, yet Europe is falling 
behind due to high vehicle prices and insufficient charging infrastructure. Meanwhile, 
Chinese  EV  manufacturers,  thanks  to  scale  and  cost  advantages,  are  becoming 
increasingly dominant. Already, one-quarter of EVs sold in the EU are Chinese. 

In response, the EU has introduced: 

  Anti-subsidy tariffs on Chinese EVs 
  A Clean Industrial Deal (CID) to support domestic manufacturing 
  The Automotive Action Plan to reinforce CID objectives 

However, despite existing policy activity, more cohesive and assertive measures are 
needed. Bruegel plans to release a policy brief following the EU-China Summit in July 
2025. 

2. Bruegel Policy Brief  

A. Cecilia Trasi (Research analyst)  

Europe’s  structural  dilemma:  EU  automakers  are  constrained  by  cost  and  scale 
limitations, while Chinese companies are filling the production gap through exports and 
foreign  direct  investment  (FDI).  In  2024  alone,  Chinese  greenfield  investments  in 
Europe’s EV and battery sectors reached €5 billion, accounting for half of all such FDI. 

Key projects include: 

  CATL battery plants in Hungary and Germany 
  BYD plant (announced) in Hungary 
  Chery’s reuse of the former Nissan site in Spain 

These investments bring benefits: 

  Addressing  the  EU’s battery  production  shortfall  (goal:  550 GWh by  2030; 

current: ~200 GWh) 

  Job creation (e.g., 9000 jobs from CATL in Hungary) 
  Helping EU firms narrow the innovation gap, especially in high-density battery 

tech 

But risks abound: 

 
  Strategic dependency on China: controls over lithium, cobalt, nickel, and rare 

earths 

  Market  distortion:  Chinese  firms  receive  vastly  higher  public  subsidies 

(average 4.5% of revenues vs. OECD’s 0.69%) 

  Cybersecurity and data vulnerability from software-defined EVs 
  Low value capture: risk of Europe becoming a site for assembly only, without 

R&D or upstream control 

EU's current approach  instrument-rich but strategy-poor. A consistent, EU-wide 
policy on foreign investment is lacking: 

  FDI  screening  is  national,  with  no  unified  EU  framework  for  greenfield 

investments. 

  Examples of divergence: 

o  Hungary: open and welcoming, little due diligence 
o  Lithuania: outright bans on China-related FDI 
o  Sweden: cautious and selective 

Key instruments and their shortcomings: 

  CVDs (countervailing duties): burdensome and reactive 
  Foreign  Subsidies  Regulation  (FSR):  good  potential  but  risks  deterring  all 

investment 

  State aid: not always tied to value-added outcomes 
  EU Battery Regulation: slow rollout, difficult implementation 
  GDPR  and  NIS2:  insufficient  to  tackle  real-time  cybersecurity  threats  from 

connected vehicles 

B. Gregor Sebastian (Senior Analyst China Corporate Advisory team, Rhodium 
Group) 

EU  must  not  block  Chinese  investment  blindly,  but  use  smart,  conditional  policy 
mechanisms.  

Recommendations:  

  Leverage  the  EU  market:  Use  its  size  to  enforce  non-price  criteria  (e.g., 

sustainability, innovation requirements) 

  Reform state aid: Condition EU public financing on strategic outcomes like IP 

sharing and local R&D 

  Revise FSR rules: Improve transparency and reinvest recovered subsidies into 

an EU EV Innovation Fund 

  Cybersecurity  policy:  Make  it  dynamic,  ensuring  regular  tech-specific  risk 

reassessment and possibly EU operational control in critical systems 

Need  clear  conditionalities,  e.g.,  EU-sourced  components,  local  workforce  hiring, 
and joint R&D initiatives. 

3.  Arthur  Corbin  (Business  Adviser,  Cabinet  of  Executive  Vice-President 
Stéphane Séjourné, EU Commission) 

“Industrial–trade–climate” trilemma    Europe  must  reconcile  open  markets  with 
industrial self-interest and climate targets. Chinese overcapacity (partly due to huge 
state  subsidies)  is  not  limited  to  EVs    similar  patterns  are  seen  in  other  sectors 
(chemicals and steel). 

Key concerns: 

  EU firms are locked out of Chinese markets, while China enjoys full EU access 
  A “price war” in China means firms there are pushing aggressively into Europe 
  EU  now  needs  a  balanced  policy  of  openness  and  protection,  including 

potential public procurement restrictions to favor domestic producers 

4. William Todts (Executive Director, transport & environment (T&E)) 

China  has  a  critical  role  in  making  clean  tech  affordable  but  supply  chain 
monopolization. While the US IRA model (Inflation Reduction Act) provides resilience 
incentives  and  fosters  domestic  innovation  through  subsidies  and  conditional 
collaboration, Europe’s reluctance to adopt a similar path is costing it. 

  Europe must support clean tech scale-up at home with a similar framework 
  Cooperation, including with Chinese firms, can be positive if it's balanced 
  The  EU  must  act  boldly,  using  state  aid  and  trade  tools  to  create  a  strategic 

industrial base
