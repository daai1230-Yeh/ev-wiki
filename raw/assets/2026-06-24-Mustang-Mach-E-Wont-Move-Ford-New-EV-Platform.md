# The Mustang Mach-E Won't Move To Ford's New EV Platform

**Author**: Andrei Nedelea | **Date**: June 23, 2026

---

The Mach-E helped make Ford a real EV contender. But the company's new plan may leave it without a direct successor. Ford confirms new UEV platform won't be used in the Mach-E, casting doubt over a direct successor.

The Ford Mustang Mach-E is in a weird spot right now. It's one of the few early EVs from a legacy automaker that made buyers reconsider getting a Tesla. Since Ford pulled the plug on the F-150 Lightning, it's been holding down Ford's North American EV fort on its own.

Even though it didn't come close to dethroning the Tesla Model Y, the Mach-E has been fairly successful. Still, it is getting a bit long in the tooth now—it was introduced almost seven years ago—and its year-over-year U.S. sales through May fell by nearly half to less than 10,000 units. The end of the federal tax credit certainly factors in there.

The Mustang Mach-E will not move to Ford's new Universal EV (UEV) platform, the lower-cost architecture that is supposed to underpin the next phase of Ford's electric vehicle strategy, Ford says in a Q&A section on its site.

"Will Ford's Universal EV platform be used for future Mustang Mach-E?" one question reads. In response, Ford says that "No, it will not be used for the Mustang Mach-E. This platform was built from a clean sheet to maximize vehicle efficiency."

None of this means that Ford plans to kill the Mach-E, or that the nameplate will disappear. However, it still casts doubt over what its future looks like. Ford could still give the Mach-E a next generation based on its current architecture. But Ford is pinning its EV future, across models, to the UEV architecture.

Ford's UEV architecture isn't just for pickups. It's designed to support SUVs and vans too. And while it's not an 800-volt platform, it should still bring improvements in all areas that matter compared to the GE1 platform that underpins the Mach-E.

In an appearance on the Rapid Response podcast, Ford CEO Jim Farley said he considered Ford's first-gen EVs—the F-150 Lightning and Mustang Mach-E—were "designed the wrong way." He pointed out that a Model Y's wiring harness was 70% lighter and a full 1 mile shorter, all because it was a clean-sheet design.

The switch to UEV doesn't automatically mean there won't be a next-gen Mach-E. Ford could use another manufacturer's platform, as it did with Volkswagen's MEB, which underpins two crossovers for the European market. It has also tapped Renault to build Ford-badged EVs on its platforms.
