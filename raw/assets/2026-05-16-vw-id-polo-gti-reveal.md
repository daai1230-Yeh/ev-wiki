# VW Wants Its First Electric GTI To Feel Old-School

**Author**: Andrei Nedelea | **Date**: May 15, 2026

---

It may not have a combustion engine under the hood, but the VW ID. Polo GTI looks, feels, and sounds like an old-school hot hatch.

VW's first electric GTI leans hard on classic design hot hatch cues. It gets GTI hardware like an electronic limited-slip differential, but its 6.8-sec sprint isn't class-leading. Fake engine sound is standard; fake gears are coming in the hotter Clubsport.

Volkswagen wants you to know that its new ID. Polo GTI is "a real GTI." It's the first time VW has used the GTI badge on an electric vehicle, but aside from the absence of a combustion engine, the traditional hot hatch formula remains unchanged. The ID. Polo GTI looks the part with an aggressive front fascia, a deep chin spoiler, vertical daytime running lights, unique GTI-badged side skirts, and a chunky rear diffuser that makes it 2 inches (5 cm) longer than the standard car. The unique 19-inch wheels with red GTI logos come standard, as do red brake calipers.

Inside, grippy bucket seats feature a reinterpretation of the traditional tartan pattern, along with a unique two-spoke steering wheel flat on the top and bottom. Red contrast details and unique graphics complete the visual makeover.

Powering the front wheels is a single 223-horsepower electric motor with 214 lb-ft (290 Nm) of torque — the same as the Cupra Raval Vz. The benchmark sprint time is 6.8 seconds to 62 mph and top speed is 109 mph (175 km/h). It earns enthusiast points with its electronically limited-slip differential, lowered suspension, and bigger brakes.

Volkswagen has included a combustion-style acceleration sound that can be activated both inside and out. The spicier Clubsport variant coming later will get more power and fake gears to shift for extra driver engagement, with paddle shifters on the steering wheel.

Weighing just 3,395 lbs (1,540 kg), the ID. Polo GTI is fairly light for an EV. It has a 52 kWh battery pack with WLTP range of 263 miles (424 km). It charges at up to 105 kW, good for 10–80% in 24 minutes.

Priced from "just under €39,000" in Germany, it's priced to match rivals like the Alpine A290 or the Peugeot E-208 GTi.
