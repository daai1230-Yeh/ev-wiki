# 2027 BMW iX3 Video Review: BMW Is Back

**Author**: Andrei Nedelea | **Date**: Updated May 18, 2026

---

BMW has spent years telling us "Neue Klasse" would be its EV reset. The first production EV suggests that it wasn't just marketing.

The new iX3 enters one of the contentious segments in the EV world: premium midsize SUVs, where being merely good gets you forgotten. The Audi Q6 E-Tron is deeply competent, the Porsche Macan Electric is properly sorted, the Mercedes-Benz GLC EV is very convincing, and the Tesla Model Y still lords over the class with its annoying blend of usefulness, software, and value.

On paper, the iX3 arrives loaded for bear. It rides on a new bespoke EV platform, promises standout range and efficiency, and it finally feels like an EV conceived from the ground up to be electric. The iX3 is also truly excellent to drive, impressively efficient in real-world use, and much easier to like inside than its odd screen setup initially suggests.

It has a few typical BMW quirks, but it is the first Neue Klasse model, soon followed by a second, and it seems worth the wait.

## Looks Odd. Works Better Than You Think

The iX3 is one of those designs that improves the longer you stare at it. The shark-nose front end is the main event, and once your eyes make peace with that, the rest of the design settles down nicely. The rear looks planted and sporty, and from some angles, it has the sort of visual confidence BMW has been missing lately.

The interior is where the real break with tradition happens. The door handles sit flush, motor out when the car unlocks, and at night they glow with a full LED halo. They use a pressure sensor and an electronic popper to unlatch the door — but if you pull harder, you can open them mechanically.

There is plenty of room inside. It feels almost as big as an X5, with a flat floor in the back, making it realistic to carry three passengers in the second row. The author (six feet tall) had no trouble fitting behind his own driving position with knee room to spare.

The parallelogram-shaped central display is easy to use, running BMW's latest OS X — quick, clear, and mostly intuitive. It's the first larger BMW without an iDrive controller. The panoramic display that stretches from pillar to pillar under the windshield is a combination of a traditional screen and a head-up display, and it's a highlight.

## The Numbers Look Good

BMW says the iX3 should be really efficient for such a big and heavy SUV. The dual-motor, big-battery xDrive 50 tester weighed 5,037 lbs, yet it had no problem returning almost 3.7 miles/kWh (17 kWh/100 km) in the city at fairly low speeds. That's better than any other electric SUV in its class, courtesy of BMW's sixth-generation electric powertrain and batteries.

Pair that with the 108.7-kWh battery pack and the result is a claimed WLTP range of 500 miles (805 km). Expect over 400 miles EPA. In real-world use, around 380 miles (611 km) is believable.

The iX3 is the fastest-charging EV from a European manufacturer. Its 800-volt platform allows it to take up to 400 kilowatts, bringing the battery from 10% to 80% in 21 minutes with an average charging power of over 230 kW. Charging for 10 minutes in optimal conditions adds 217 miles (350 km).

It feels quicker than its claimed 0-60 mph time of 4.7 seconds suggests, with 469 hp and 475 lb-ft (645 Nm) and continues pulling hard even at higher speeds before topping out at 130 mph. Most motivation comes from the rear synchronous motor (326 hp, 320 lb-ft). The front motor contributes 167 hp and 188 lb-ft when you floor it or under low-grip conditions.

## It Drives Like A BMW

However futuristic it may look parked up, it quickly feels familiar from behind the wheel — familiar in the specifically BMW sense: a firm edge to the ride, real body control, and an eagerness to change direction. This is something that China can't yet replicate, no matter how technologically advanced its EVs are.

Air suspension isn't offered and adaptive dampers won't arrive until later. The chassis feels happy taking corners quickly and is unexpectedly enjoyable on a good road. For a fairly large electric SUV, the iX3 shrinks around you in a way that very few rivals can match.

BMW hails the Heart of Joy driving control unit as a bit of a revolution, with "10x quicker" responses. Traction control intervention is more precise, and it almost feels like the car knows what you're about to do beforehand. The soft stop / limo stop function eliminates that final jolt of whiplash, working with regenerative braking, on inclines, and around bends.

It also has excellent advanced driver assistance systems. The iX3 is very smooth when steering itself on any road, and you can initiate an automatic lane change merely by glancing into the side mirror. Self-parking shows a progress indicator and inspires confidence.

## The Hype Is Deserved

Once you get past the styling and the unusual cabin layout, the iX3 reveals itself as one of the most complete EVs BMW has ever built. It's smooth, quiet, quick enough to feel properly expensive, and efficient enough to make some rivals look a bit wasteful.

More importantly, it has the one thing BMW absolutely had to get right here: it feels engineered by people who still care how a car drives. The iX3 is more than just another premium electric SUV — it's BMW finally showing that its EV future need not be a compromise among technology, efficiency, and driver appeal.

Some Chinese rivals still offer more wow-factor per euro, and Tesla still wins on ecosystem advantages, but as a polished, genuinely satisfying all-rounder, the iX3 feels like BMW getting its groove back at exactly the right time.
