US tariffs cause Dutch automotive industry

suppliers to lose billions

Dutch companies that supply parts to the automotive industry will suffer greatly from the new
US import tariffs on European cars and parts. This year and next year alone, the tariffs will cause
the industry to lose around €2.1 billion, according to calculations by Rabobank economists.

US President Donald Trump had already announced the 25 per cent levy. It is part of a larger
package of trade measures that he will present on Wednesday at around 21.00 hours CET.

The additional taxes are expected to reduce the demand for European cars, which will have
consequences for the entire sector. Large-scale car production in the Netherlands came to an end
last year with the departure of BMW from the VDL Nedcar car factory in the Dutch village of
Born. However, there is still a group of mostly medium-sized and small companies that produce
parts for car manufacturers in Germany and France, among other countries, and also directly for
the US market. In addition, the chemical industry — for example, plastic and rubber
manufacturers — supplies mostly German car manufacturers.

The export of car parts to the United States amounted to $110 million in 2023. By comparison, in
the same year, the Netherlands exported $1.1 billion worth of cars and almost $1 billion worth of
car parts to Germany, according to calculations by Rabobank economists. This means that the
Netherlands will suffer mainly from so-called second-order economic effects. ‘You have to look
at the entire network. A shockwave will spread throughout the entire chain’, says economist
Hugo Erken. ‘Moreover, the car industry in Germany is very important for the country’s entire
economy, and Germany is the Netherlands’ most important trading partner.’

The companies themselves also foresee an effect from the trade tariffs, but say they are unable to
make any predictions about the exact consequences. Rolflex, which makes doors for cars and
other products, says it is assessing the situation. ‘It is rather premature, but we are already
focusing on different markets in different countries. There is always a segment that will continue.
It’s good to consider our options’, says marketing manager Maureen Bannink.

A spokesperson for chip machine manufacturer NXP agrees. ‘We are keeping a close eye on the
American tariffs to quickly determine what consequences they may have for our company and
our customers.’ The company mainly supplies chips to companies that build parts for the
automotive industry, such as dashboards.

Other examples of Dutch companies that produce for the automotive sector are tyre manufacturer
Apollo Vredestein, roof system supplier Inalfa and Bons & Evers, which supplies aluminium,
copper and brass parts to the sector. For the time being, both the tyre manufacturer and Bons &
Evers claim to be exempt from the direct tariff. The companies have not provided a comment on
the indirect effects. In addition to manufacturing companies, there are also many Dutch trading
companies active in the automotive industry.

Rabobank calls the effect of €2.1 billion in lost revenue due to the import tax over the next two
years ‘limited’. According to the researchers, this does not alter the fact that individual Dutch
sectors and companies will suffer greatly.

‘It is clear that the automotive sector cannot afford this new blow’, says Erken of Rabobank. The
German automotive industry is already struggling with declining production numbers and last
year Dutch companies also saw the last large-scale car manufacturer leave the Netherlands. ‘This
caused a visible decline in production numbers in the Netherlands, and it is likely that suppliers
were also affected.’

A concrete example of a Dutch company that has had to cut its workforce due to the economic
malaise in the European car industry is Apollo Vredestein. Some sixty people, 10 per cent of all
employees, will have to leave, the company announced on Tuesday.

Rabobank predicts that in the long term, the economic damage of the levy on European cars in
the Netherlands will amount to some €600 million annually. The expectation is that the economy
will adjust to the new situation.

‘Companies can look for new markets, although the question remains whether they can charge
the same price in other countries’, says Erken. In addition, moving production to the US is an
option, but according to the economist, that is not an easy decision. ‘Certainly because
entrepreneurs do not know whether the tariffs will remain in effect for years to come’.
