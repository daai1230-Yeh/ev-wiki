# The 2026 Toyota C-HR Feels Like A Misstep In A Pivotal Year For EVs

**Author**: Suvrat Kothari | **Date**: 2026-05-21

*The C-HR is a fun EV wrapped in an outdated experience. It proves that new doesn't mean compelling.*

---

| Spec | Value |

## Overview

Toyota axed the C-HR from its U.S. lineup in 2022 after sluggish sales, but the car is reborn as a fully electric avatar. The C-HR is now a small electric crossover, smaller than the bZ, and comes as an all-wheel-drive subcompact electric crossover. After a week-long test, the C-HR felt plenty of fun from behind the wheel — adequate real-world range and competitive fast-charging speeds. But the infotainment feels archaic, and the cabin's ergonomics have plenty of room for improvement.

## Range & Efficiency

The C-HR gets 74.7 kWh of battery capacity, delivering 287 miles of EPA range (SE trim) or 273 miles (XSE tested). Highway testing showed 3.7 miles/kWh, climbing to 5.0 miles/kWh in the city.

## Charging

At an Ionna charging station (400 kW capable), the C-HR pulled a maximum of 135 kW. Key results:
- 15–80%: 26 minutes (adding 191 miles)
- 15–100%: 48 minutes
- The charging curve is flat until ~60%, then gradually tapers
- Compare: Hyundai Ioniq 5 does 10–80% in 20 minutes with 800V/350 kW architecture

## Performance & Handling

- 338 hp + AWD → 0–60 in 4.9 seconds, strong for this segment
- Rides on the stiffer side at city speeds; expansion joints caused loud thuds
- Handling is engaging and surprisingly nimble for a crossover; steering lacks feedback
- The bZ, Ioniq 5, and Nissan Leaf all ride with better composure

## Technology & Infotainment — The Big Problem

Despite launching alongside the new RAV4, the C-HR gets an **older** infotainment system. The new RAV4 and Lexus ES run Toyota's Arene software platform (responsive, with native EV route planning). The C-HR gets none of that:
- Slow to boot, occasionally laggy 14-inch touchscreen
- No native EV routing — relies on Apple CarPlay / Android Auto
- Steering wheel partially blocks the gauge cluster (reviewer needed painkillers from craning neck)
- Toyota confirmed current C-HR lacks hardware/software to support Arene platform

## Verdict

At $38,595, the C-HR competes with the Tesla Model 3, Ioniq 5, Equinox EV, Bolt, Leaf, and bZ. Its strengths — competitive range, decent charging, fun handling — are undermined by:

- Archaic infotainment vs. class
- No native EV route planning
- Stiffer ride vs. competitors
- Close in price to the larger, more capable Ioniq 5 and Equinox EV

The Bolt and Leaf start $8K+ cheaper and offer more modern native Google infotainment. Even a 4-year-old used Model 3 or Kia EV6 will feel more modern than the C-HR. The C-HR feels like Toyota's EV focus remains a side quest while hybrids stay the priority.
