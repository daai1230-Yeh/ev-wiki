# InsideEVs Critical Materials: The Future of EV Charging

**Authors**: Tim Levin, Suvrat Kothari, Rob Stumpf, Mack Hogan | **Date**: May 15–16, 2026

---

## Plug In: Faster Charging For Everyone

A new trend is emerging in U.S. EV charging: companies are rolling out hardware capable of delivering 500 kW, 600 kW, or up to a full megawatt, preparing for electric big rigs and next-generation EVs.

Recent milestones: ChargePoint revealed a 600 kW "world's fastest standalone EV charger"; ABB unveiled plans for 1.2 MW chargers; Kempower unveiled a charger with a Megawatt Charging System connector doing 1.2 MW plus a 560 kW CCS plug; Italian firm Alpitronic launched chargers delivering up to 1,000 kW to semis and 600 kW to passenger EVs (rolling out in the U.S. early next year). Tesla is gradually rolling out 500 kW V4 Superchargers.

There is a catch: no passenger EV in the U.S. currently accepts over 500 kW. The fastest-charging U.S. EVs — Lucid Gravity, Porsche Cayenne Electric, BMW iX3 — are capped at 400 kW. Still, charging companies are future-proofing their networks.

"Some of the high-power Chinese cars may find their way to the U.S. in the next five years, so these higher-power capable chargers future-proof them," said Loren McDonald, CEO of Chargeonomics. "We might also see some 500 kW capable BEVs in the next few years in the U.S."

Ionna CEO Seth Cutler: "We are evaluating higher power. I think it's just a question of timing — when does it make sense to make those changes."

## One More Thing: EV Consideration Still Surprisingly High

Despite the end of federal tax credits and slowing EV sales, consumer interest in EVs remains robust. JD Power's Brent Gruber says 26% of car shoppers called themselves "very likely" to consider an EV in April — up 3 percentage points month-over-month. The "very unlikely" segment fell 4 points to 18%.

Key driver: gas now costs over $4.50/gallon, up from under $3 before the Iran conflict, shifting attitudes.

Yet actual BEV market share is only 6–7% in 2026, down from ~8% in 2024–2025. The gap is largely explained by affordability (especially for younger, interested buyers), perception gaps on charging infrastructure (already denser than most believe), and dealerships doing less EV education since tax credits expired.
