# GM advances LMR battery cells as LFP plans fade

**Author**: Cora Werwitzke | **Date**: June 12, 2026

---

General Motors is reassessing its battery strategy, pivoting from LFP toward lithium manganese-rich (LMR) technology for future EVs.

## Key Points

- GM is now prioritising **LMR (lithium manganese-rich)** cells over previously planned LFP chemistry
- LMR offers ~**33% higher energy density** than LFP at comparable cost
- GM's Spring Hill (Tennessee) plant will produce LFP cells, but for **stationary energy storage**, not EVs
- LMR cells expected to be market-ready by **2028**, primarily for GM pickups and large SUVs
- GM and LG Energy Solution have been collaborating on LMR since 2015; LG holds 200+ LMR patents

## Battery Strategy History

GM has pivoted multiple times:
1. Initially: Nickel-based NMCA (nickel-cobalt-manganese-aluminium) cells
2. Then: Planned LFP conversion for Spring Hill plant
3. Now: Refocusing on LMR as the "workhorse" technology

> "There is a possibility where LFP does not earn its way into our portfolio," said Kurt Kelty, GM's head of battery development. "That's where we're going to be using the big volume." (on LMR)

## LMR Technology Overview

LMR uses a cathode with high manganese content. Manganese is significantly cheaper than cobalt, reducing costs. However, challenges remain:
- Limited durability
- High capacity loss over time
- Restricted fast-charging performance

Future LMR cell production could rely on domestic US supplies of lithium, graphite, and manganese — a strategic advantage.

## Current EV Fleet

Most current GM EVs use nickel-rich batteries. The exception is the recently introduced Chevrolet Bolt, which uses LFP cells from CATL (China).
