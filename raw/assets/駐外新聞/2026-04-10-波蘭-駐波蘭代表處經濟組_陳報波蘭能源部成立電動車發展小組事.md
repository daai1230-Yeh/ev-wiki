Developing  legislative  solutions  and  removing  barriers  to  investment  and  charging

which has begun work under the Ministry of Energy. This new formula for cooperation 

between  the  administration  and  the  market  is  intended  to  translate  into  concrete 

solutions for investors and users. 

Joint actions to accelerate change

in nature and focused on specific, implementable solutions.

The Electromobility Development Team at the Ministry of Energy – what does it 

do and what is the role of PIRE? 

April 2, 2026 

The  Electromobility  Development  Team  at  the  Ministry  of  Energy  is  a  formally 

appointed subsidiary body of the Minister of Energy, established by order of March 3, 

2026  (effective  March  4,  2026).  Its  task  is  not  to  "market  electromobility,"  but  to 

organize  and  accelerate  regulatory  and  implementation  work  that  will  effectively 

remove barriers to the zero-emission vehicle market and alternative fuel infrastructure 

in Poland. 

Why was the Electromobility Development Team established at the Ministry of Energy? 

There are three main reasons for establishing this Team – each stemming from specific 

market needs and regulatory obligations, rather than general declarations. 

1) Poland must quickly adapt its regulations to EU law 

The order explicitly states that the Team is to review and analyze existing national and 

EU  regulations  regarding  electromobility  and  alternative  fuels,  and  identify  areas 

requiring changes or adjustments. This is a practical response to a situation where the 

pace  of  change  in  the  EU  (infrastructure,  technical  standards,  grid  requirements)  is 

faster than the updating of national procedures and regulations. 

2) The market has "procedural" and "technical" barriers that block investments 

The team's goal is to identify legal, technical, and organizational barriers hindering the 

development of the zero-emission vehicle market and alternative fuel infrastructure. In 

practice, this includes lengthy and inconsistent administrative procedures, connection 

issues, and technical standards that can delay charging investments by months. 

3) We need a group of industry experts in one place 

The composition of the Team demonstrates that the Ministry of Energy wanted to bring 

together institutions and organizations that have a real impact on implementation: from 

3 

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
the administration (climate, transport), through the Energy Regulatory Office (URE), 

the Office of Technical Inspection (UDT), the Transport and Logistics Administration 

(TDT), and the State Fire Service (PSP), to market organizations and representatives of 

the  transport,  logistics,  and  logistics  sector.  The  Polish  Chamber  of  Electromobility 

Development also plays a key advisory role – as a non-governmental organization, we 

are fully engaged in the ministry's work on the e-mobility sector. 

What does the Electromobility Development Team do: scope of work step by step 
The order describes in detail the Team's tasks and how it is to operate in practice. This 

is important because it provides a clear point of reference: it precisely explains what 

the Team for Electromobility Development at the Ministry of Energy does, what topics 

it addresses, and what recommendations it is expected to prepare. This ensures that the 

Team's  description  is  based  on  specific  provisions  and  facts,  rather  than  general 

declarations. This offers a simple benefit to the reader: it immediately becomes clear 

what market problems (law, procedures, technical barriers) can be solved by the Team's 

work, and what companies can realistically expect from its activities. 

1.  Review and analysis of national and EU regulations regarding electromobility and 

alternative fuels + identification of areas for change/adaptation. 

2. 

Identifying legal, technical and organizational barriers hindering the development 

of the zero-emission vehicle market and infrastructure. 

3.  Developing  improvements  for  infrastructure 

investments  –  especially  in 

administrative and connection procedures and technical standards. 

4.  Developing recommendations and assumptions for amending the provisions of the 
Act on Electromobility and Alternative Fuels (or for a new act in this regard). 
5.  Preparing  recommendations  regarding  the  timetable  for  implementing  the 

regulations and the necessary enforcement actions. 

How the Ministry of Energy is working on electromobility issues 
This is a very practical workflow: first, the Team identifies what is actually blocking 

the development of electromobility (e.g., regulations, procedures, and standards), then 

prepares  proposals  for  specific  changes,  and  finally  finalizes  the  implementation 

process—that is, determining the order and date in which these changes should take 

effect and what implementing regulations are required. This means that the work doesn't 

end with analysis, but rather leads to solutions that can be implemented in practice on 

the market. 

The cost context is essential in the work of the Electromobility Development Team, as

determine the profitability of fleet decisions and the pace of transport electrification. 

This factor directly impacts TCO, budget planning, and the need to invest in charging 

infrastructure as a tool for stabilizing operating costs. We discuss this in more detail in 

a separate PIRE article on how the conflict in the Middle East is affecting fuel prices in 

Europe and Poland and the cost risk for companies. 

The  Electromobility  Development  Team  at  the  Ministry  of  Energy  is  working: 

procedure, protocols, reports 
The team works in person or remotely. Decisions are made by consensus, or if this is 

not possible, by resolution (with the chairperson having the casting vote in the event of 

a tie). Important organizational considerations: 

  minutes are taken of the meetings, 
 

the service is provided by the Department of Electromobility, Fuels and Hydrogen 

Economy at the Ministry of Energy, 

  The team is obliged to submit a report to the Minister by 30 January each year for 

the previous year. 

This is important for the market because the Team's work has a fixed schedule and is 

accounted for based on specific results - it is not a one-time initiative that ends with a 

meeting and a press release. 

The composition of the Electromobility Team and the advisory role of PIRE: what 

does this mean in practice? 
The order explicitly names a representative of the Polish Chamber of Electromobility 

Development Association among the Team members . This is crucial because it places 

PIRE  in  a  formal  advisory  structure  with  the  Minister  of  Energy  –  a  place  where 

recommendations  for  regulations,  standards,  and  implementation  schedules  are 
developed. 

In practice, the role of PIRE in the Team is advisory, but very specific: 

  we  bring  a  market  perspective  (infrastructure  producers,  operators,  fleets,  TSL 

sector), 

  we  help  "translate"  investment  barriers  into  the  language  of  legislative  and 

procedural recommendations, 

  We  ensure  that  regulations  and  schedules  are  feasible  in  the  market  realities

(capacity, connections, technical standards, costs). 

The ordinance also allows for the participation of non-Team members in an advisory 

capacity,  provided  their  expertise  is  useful  for  carrying  out  the  tasks.  This  creates  a 

space for the Team's work to be informed by expert input, depending on the topic (e.g., 

HDV infrastructure, safety standards, connections, network fees, tariff models). 

Why is this important for the market: what do companies realistically expect from 

the Team's work? 
From the perspective of entrepreneurs, one thing matters: whether the Team's work will 

translate into tangible improvements that will be felt in investments and operating costs. 

Companies don't need another "platform for dialogue"—they need simpler procedures, 

clear rules, and predictable deadlines. 

In practice, the market will look at effects such as: 

 

shorter  and  more  predictable  investment  processes  for  charging  stations  (fewer 

discrepancies between offices, fewer bottlenecks along the way), 

  more consistent technical standards and guidelines for infrastructure (especially 

high-capacity commercial facilities and heavy transport), 

 

improvements  in  grid connections and better coordination between the investor 

and the distribution system operator, 

  better laws and implementing acts that respond to real market problems instead of 

creating further ambiguities, 

 

a clear implementation schedule, thanks to which companies can plan CAPEX and 

TCO of fleets without continuous day-to-day operation. 

If these elements begin to work, investments in electromobility will cease to be a "trial" 

project and will become a normal business decision—including in the TSL sector and 
large fleets. This is crucial, because market development today doesn't stop at vehicle 

technology alone: the biggest delays are generated by procedures, connections, a lack 

of clear standards, and uncertainty about deadlines. The Electromobility Development 

Team at the Ministry of Energy can truly change the pace of the market if it consistently 

removes barriers that block charging investments while simultaneously implementing 

regulations that are operationally feasible (for the investor, the grid operator, and the 

facility manager). Then, companies will begin to make decisions based on numbers—

energy costs, cost per kilometer, infrastructure availability—rather than on formal risk 

and "unknowns" in the schedule. And as formal risk decreases, scale grows faster: more 

6 

 
 
 
 
 
 
projects,  greater  competition,  better  service  availability,  and,  ultimately,  faster 

development of electromobility in Poland. 

Why is the Electromobility Team at the Ministry of Energy important to us? 
For PIRE, it's  crucial that the Electromobility Development Team  at  the Ministry of 

Energy creates a formal space where a "market barometer" can directly translate into 

systemic decisions. Our mission is not only to promote electromobility, but above all, 

to organize knowledge, identify barriers, and develop solutions that make investments 

in  zero-emission vehicles and infrastructure operationally  feasible and commercially 

viable.  In  practice,  this  means  consistently  bringing  the  perspectives  of  fleets, 

infrastructure operators, and the TSL sector to the Team's work: where procedures are 

blocking investments, what technical standards should be clarified, how to accelerate 

connection, and how to build an implementation schedule that will allow companies to 

plan CAPEX and TCO without working in the dark. This allows the Team to become a 

tool  for  real  change:  from  diagnosing  market  problems,  through  legislative 

recommendations, to solutions that the market can implement at scale. 

It's also worth remembering that the Team's work concerns not only infrastructure and 

"technical" regulations, but also the direction in which the fleet market is heading across 

the EU. Requirements and practices related to clean corporate vehicles are increasingly 

shaping  companies'  purchasing  policies,  reporting  standards,  and  expectations  of 

transport service providers, which directly impacts the pace of fleet electrification in 

Poland.  We  expand  on  this  context  in  a  separate  PIRE  article  on    clean  corporate 

vehicles in Poland and EU fleets, which demonstrates how EU regulations and targets 

translate into fleet decisions and investment planning for vehicles and charging. 

What  PIRE  brings  to  the  Team  and  why  it  strengthens  our  role  as  a  "market 

barometer" 
At PIRE, we consistently act as a barometer of electromobility in Poland: we present 
data, real costs, implementation barriers, and conclusions about "what needs to change 

to accelerate the market." Our presence in the Electromobility Development Team at 

the  Ministry  of  Energy  means  that  these  market  observations  can  more  quickly  be 

translated into recommendations and systemic solutions – including those crucial for 

fleets and the transport, forwarding, and logistics sectors. 

It is also a natural place to connect threads from our other publications – for example, 

the report "Project Zero: decarbonising transport without slogans – lessons for fleets 

and TSL", which shows how to make decarbonisation decisions based on numbers, not
