# "500 city buses have already been electrified" – Hendryk Münster, Hamburger Hochbahn

**Author**: Carla Westerheide | **Date**: April 23, 2026

---

"Ultimately, conventional vehicle technology was much simpler than electric buses, but we have mastered both." With this level of confidence, Hendryk Münster reflects on a decade of electrification at Hamburger Hochbahn. The results are tangible: of around 1,200 city buses, nearly 500 are already electric.

Hamburger Hochbahn has emerged as one of Europe's leading examples of large-scale bus electrification. With around 500 battery-electric buses already in operation out of a fleet of 1,200 vehicles, the company has moved well beyond pilot projects and into industrial-scale deployment. The operator is also expanding its depot infrastructure, with more than 750 electrified parking bays expected by the end of 2026.

Hendryk Münster, who has been responsible for the electrification programme for around a decade, outlines how the transition was implemented in close coordination with local grid operators. A key focus is the integration of stationary battery storage, initially tested within the KoLa (coordination function and load management) research project and now part of daily operations. Beyond stabilising depot operations, the system enables participation in energy markets and optimisation of procurement strategies.

On battery storage, Münster said: "If you ask me, I would say that, given the current state of the art, it is essential to engage with the topic and definitely factor it in. We can operate this battery storage system in a way that pays off and has a clearly positive effect on running such an electric network."

On the future of depot integration: "In the future, the depot will definitely be more integrated into the overall urban energy network, and I believe it will be significantly optimised. Efficiencies will increase, charging technology will become more efficient, energy transmission will become more efficient and more transparent. Energy management will play a major role, as will economic optimisation."

Regarding whether buses should also perform bidirectional charging, Münster explained that the company prefers using stationary battery storage systems rather than using buses for V2G. "Our core task is to transport passengers from A to B. These vehicles are optimised for operation on routes with very optimised deployment times and rotations and are therefore not primarily available for bidirectional charging. A battery storage system, on the other hand, is available 24/7 and therefore offers significantly greater potential."

His advice for operators just starting to electrify: "Talk to your energy supplier, find solutions together, address your issues, bring them along on the journey and be bold."
