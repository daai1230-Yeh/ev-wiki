# Mazda's EV Delay Is Barely A Pivot

**Author**: Mack Hogan | **Date**: May 12, 2026

---

Mazda's first long-range, bespoke-platform EV — originally promised for 2027 — has been pushed back to 2029 or later, according to a report in Automotive News based on the company's May 12 earnings call. CEO Masahiro Moro acknowledged that no facilities had been impaired or written off as part of the delay, telling analysts the call had been made "before we started" and that the company had "always been careful" with battery EVs. In practice, that suggests no retooling, no firm supplier contracts, and nothing else that could not be unwound at zero cost.

For context, Mazda's only U.S. EV to date is the MX-30, which arrived in 2021 with 100 miles of EPA range at $35,000 and was withdrawn from the market within roughly two years. With this latest postponement, the author argues Mazda is barely retreating — because it never really advanced. Resources will instead shift toward internal-combustion and hybrid development, including a CX-5 hybrid built on Mazda's own in-house system rather than the Toyota-derived hardware used in the CX-50 Hybrid. More hybrids are expected before the end of the decade.

Mazda now plans to invest roughly $7.52 billion in electrification by 2030 — a small share of what Ford, GM, Toyota, or Hyundai (which alone projects $26 billion on its U.S. EV supply chain by 2030) intend to commit. The author concedes that Mazda's smaller war chest justifies caution but warns that postponing the hard work of EV development tends to compound disadvantages over time. Mazda's EV efforts continue in China, where it sells well-styled models co-developed with local partners, including the CX-6e — though that vehicle is essentially a reskin of a Chinese-developed car rather than a true Mazda EV.
